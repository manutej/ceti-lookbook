Issue 07 · Page 02 From the editor A small letter before we begin
No. 01 Editor's letter CETI.AI

There is a particular kind of professional fatigue that comes from doing the same coordination work twice a week, every week, for years. It is the fatigue of pulling things together that already exist. Of looking up what you already knew. Of formatting what you already wrote.

I have spent the last three years sitting next to marketers, operators, partners, and founders while they tried to bend new software to old habits. The pattern is always the same. The first week is novelty. The second week is mild disappointment. The third week is quiet abandonment. The tool was never the problem. The frame was.

This issue is built around a different frame. We are not asking you to adopt a productivity stack, change your workflow, or pivot your team. We are asking you to imagine, plainly, that you have one additional coworker — someone patient, well-read, fast, and at the desk whenever you arrive. Someone who will not author the thing that only you can author, but who will gather, sort, draft, and reformat all the material around it so the version that takes your name on it can be the version that deserves to.

What follows are eight pages that try to give that coworker a face. A Tuesday morning, narrated twice. Six kinds of work, illustrated. A diagram of how Claude reaches into the apps your work already lives in. Three small case studies from people doing this work today. A short reflection on judgment. And, at the back, the simple pricing — because pricing has stopped being a reason to wait.

Read it as you would a magazine, not a manual. The point is the picture, not the procedure.

Yours,

A Tuesday morning

Same task, same person, same coffee. Two ways of doing it — one of which has not aged well.

The first method is how most of us still work. Tabs upon tabs, copy-paste, building tables by hand, losing the thread, watching the clock. The second is what already exists if you simply have Claude open in one of those tabs instead.

Issue 07 · Page 05 Feature essay — Tuesday morning A narrative in two hours
Essay No. 01 Tuesday morning 2,000 words, narrated

The morning Sarah stopped working twice.

A marketing lead at a fifty-person startup opens her laptop, makes a flat white, and decides — for reasons she cannot quite name — to try something different.

Sarah is forty-one, has been a marketing lead for nine years, and has the good fortune of working from a kitchen table that gets the morning light. It is a Tuesday in early spring. The flat white is still warm, the dog is already disappointed, and there is a competitor brief due to her CEO at eleven. She has done this brief, in one form or another, perhaps four hundred times. She is very good at it.

The competitor in question is a series-B company that her sales team has started losing deals to. She needs to understand them, quickly. Their pricing, their positioning, their last three product launches, the way their CEO talks on podcasts, what their customers complain about. Sarah opens her laptop the way she has opened it every Tuesday for nine years. She opens a new browser window. She types the company's name plus the word "pricing." She opens twelve tabs.

By 9:31 she has skimmed three articles, lost her place in two more, and re-read the same paragraph in a TechCrunch piece because her eye kept drifting. She switches to Notion. She makes a blank page titled Competitor Brief. She stares at it. She switches back to the browser and copy-pastes a quote, which arrives missing its formatting and with a stray hyperlink. She begins to build a comparison table by hand, which means typing column headers, then typing values, then realizing the values are inconsistent because the source articles are from different quarters.

At 10:17 she has a table that is roughly accurate and entirely ugly. She has not yet started writing the brief itself. She makes a second flat white. She loses ten minutes to an email about office snacks. She watches the clock the way she used to watch the clock in school.

The other morning

The day before, by accident, Sarah had a different Tuesday. The competitor had been someone else — a German company she had never heard of — and she had been even more behind. She had opened Claude, which her team had been quietly using for six weeks, and typed a single sentence: "I need a competitor brief on Acme by 11 a.m. Pull their public pricing, last three launches, recent customer reviews, and how their CEO talks about the category. Format it the way I formatted the brief on Beacon last quarter."

Claude had read the Beacon brief from her Drive. It had pulled from the web. It had built a comparison table as an Artifact, which sat there in the side panel like a polite intern holding a clipboard. Sarah had asked, on a hunch, "what about their enterprise tier?" and the table had updated, with two new rows and three new citations. She had asked, again, "what are their three biggest weaknesses on review sites?" and the answer had come back specific enough to be useful, hedged enough to be honest.

She had spent the rest of that hour writing. The actual brief. The judgment of what to recommend. The story about why this competitor mattered to her CEO and to the sales team. She had been — and this is the part she remembers — thinking. Not gathering. Not formatting. Not coordinating between twelve tabs. Thinking.

The handoff

Today, back in the kitchen, Sarah closes the Notion tab. She closes nine of the twelve browser tabs. She opens Claude again. She types her one sentence. She watches, this time with less surprise, as the work she would have done by 11:30 appears by 9:38. She spends the next hour writing the brief, in her voice, with her judgment. She sends it to her CEO at 10:42. She walks the dog. The kettle, by then, is cold — but it is cold in the way of a morning that ended on time, not a morning that ran out.

What changed between the two Tuesdays is not that Sarah became faster at typing. She is the same typist she has been since college. What changed is what she did with the first hour. She no longer spent it as a research analyst. She spent it as a marketing lead. The difference, multiplied across forty-seven Tuesdays a year, is the entire difference between someone who feels useful and someone who feels behind.

What you can actually do

Six kinds of work Claude does well — today, without code.

Each of these is something thousands of people are doing in Claude right now. The trick is not the tool; it is knowing which tasks to bring to it.

The inventory that follows is descriptive, not aspirational. It is what we observe when we sit next to operators, marketers, analysts, and founders during a CETI training week. We have grouped the work into six categories. The vignettes on the following pages take each category and tell a small story about it, because lists are not how work is felt.

Read it the way you might read a contents page of a magazine, not the line items of a feature comparison. The categories overlap. Real work always overlaps.

What you can actually do · In prose

Six small portraits of cowork.

Each portrait is a single person, a single task, a single Tuesday. The categories are the same. The texture is what matters.

01

The researcher

Mapping a market before the meeting begins.

Priya runs corporate development for a forty-person services firm. On a Tuesday morning she is asked, with no warning, to brief her CEO on the competitive landscape for a category they are considering entering. The category did not exist three years ago. She has ninety minutes.

She opens Claude. She asks for the eight companies operating in the space, their funding history, their stated positioning, and the three reviews she should read first for each one. Within four minutes she has a structured map with citations. She spends the rest of the ninety minutes doing what she is actually paid for: deciding which of those eight matter, and why. The map is not the brief. The map is what frees her to write the brief.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Research

A landscape, mapped in four minutes.

Map the eight companies operating in the autonomous-claims category. For each: founding year, total funding, last round, stated positioning in one sentence, and the three customer reviews I should read first. Cite each source with a working link.

Add the discovery notes from our last board meeting as context. Format the output as a table so I can paste it into Notion before eleven.

— Priya, Corp. dev. lead

02

The writer

A first draft that already sounds like the firm.

Marcus is a partner at a boutique strategy consultancy. He has a proposal due to a healthcare client tomorrow. He gives Claude two past winning proposals, the discovery notes from the client conversations, and a one-paragraph brief on what he wants the engagement to look like. Twelve minutes later he has a draft.

The draft is not finished. It is a first draft. But it is a first draft in his voice, with his structure, with his firm's vocabulary. He spends the next two hours rewriting the parts that need his judgment, sharpening the close, deleting the paragraphs that were too generic. The proposal goes out at six. He is home in time for dinner. This used to be a Saturday.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Writing

A first draft in the firm's voice.

Draft a proposal for the healthcare client whose discovery notes I just uploaded. Match the structure of the two winning proposals in this Project. Use our firm's cadence in the executive summary — numbered phases, plain verbs, no consultant-ese.

Leave the pricing section as a placeholder. Flag any claim that needs my judgment with [VERIFY]. I'll rewrite the close.

— Marcus, Partner, strategy

03

The analyst

A messy spreadsheet, made legible.

Aisha is a finance manager at a direct-to-consumer brand. The expense report she just received from her ops team is the same expense report she has been receiving for three years: nine tabs, inconsistent categories, three different formats for dates. She has historically spent half a Tuesday reconciling it before she can even begin to analyze it.

She drops the file into Claude with the message: "Reconcile this against the chart of accounts I uploaded last quarter. Flag anything that looks like a category change. Show me a clean monthly trend by department." The reconciled file appears as an Artifact. The flagged items are footnoted. The trend chart is rendered. Aisha now begins her real work, which is asking why marketing spend in February was twice what was forecast.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Analysis

A spreadsheet, made legible.

Reconcile this nine-tab expense report against the chart of accounts I uploaded last quarter. Flag anything that looks like a category change. Show me a clean monthly trend by department, with department-over-department deltas highlighted in the chart.

Surface anything that is more than twenty per cent off the trailing six-month average. I want to know what to ask my ops lead before our 2 p.m. review.

— Aisha, Finance manager, DTC

04

The organizer

A folder, turned into a system.

Diego is the chief of staff at an early-stage company. There is a folder in the shared Drive labelled Vendor Quotes — 2026 that contains forty-three PDFs from twelve vendors across three categories. Nobody, including Diego, has ever sat down and read all of them. The folder has the gravitational pull of a project that will never start.

He gives Claude permission to read the folder. He asks for a single comparison table across all vendors, with columns for price, contract length, included support, and the three most important fine-print clauses. He asks for a one-paragraph summary of each vendor's overall pitch. The table is built. The folder, for the first time in eighteen months, becomes a tool instead of a backlog.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Organize

Forty-three PDFs, one table.

Read every PDF in the Drive folder “Vendor Quotes — 2026.” Build one comparison table with columns for price, contract length, included support, and the three most important fine-print clauses per vendor. Group by category.

Write a one-paragraph summary of each vendor's pitch in plain English. Flag the two vendors that look most similar — I suspect we are negotiating with the same parent company twice.

— Diego, Chief of staff

05

The decision-maker

A structured second opinion, before the call.

Yuki is the founder of a sixty-person product company. She has a hard call to make: whether to extend an offer to a senior hire who interviewed brilliantly but whose references were uneven. She has the candidate's resume, her own notes, the panel feedback, and the reference summaries. She has, also, the discomfort of a decision she does not yet trust.

She gives Claude the documents and asks for the three strongest arguments in favour, the three strongest arguments against, and the questions she has not yet asked herself. The reply is not a recommendation. It is a structured second opinion — the kind a good advisor would give in a coffee, if she had one available on a Tuesday morning. She makes the call. She knows why.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Decide

A structured second opinion.

Read the resume, the panel feedback, my notes, and the three reference summaries I have shared. Give me the three strongest arguments in favour of an offer, the three strongest arguments against, and the five questions I have not yet asked myself.

Do not recommend. I want the second opinion a good advisor would give over coffee — specific, hedged where it should be, and honest about what is missing.

— Yuki, Founder & CEO

06

The builder

A tiny tool, ready in twenty minutes.

Esme runs operations for a fourteen-person agency. Her team has been guessing at project pricing using a spreadsheet that is technically a calculator but practically a museum piece. She does not have a developer. She does not want one.

She describes the calculator she wishes she had — inputs for scope, team size, project length, complexity multiplier; outputs for floor, target, and ceiling price. Claude builds it as an Artifact. She tests it against five recent projects. She tweaks two of the multipliers. She shares the link with her team. Twenty minutes. It is not a product. It is a tiny tool. The category is new, and it is one of the most useful things her team will use this year.

Hover for the prompt
Prompt · Build

A calculator, as an Artifact.

Build me a project-pricing calculator as an Artifact. Inputs: scope (S/M/L), team size (1–8), project length in weeks, complexity multiplier (1.0–2.5). Outputs: floor price, target price, ceiling price, and a one-line rationale per output.

Use our agency rate card from the doc I shared. Style it like a simple form — warm cream background, italic serif headings. I'll share the link with my team in ten minutes.

— Esme, Ops lead, agency

Essay No. 02 The connected workspace A business-side view of MCP

When the tool stops asking you to paste.

There is a quiet shift happening underneath the surface of every team's software. We are going to describe it without using a single acronym.

For the last twenty years, the way software has worked together at a small business is the way two people who do not share a language work together. They gesture. They point. They write notes on the backs of receipts. They paste, into one window, the thing they copied out of another. The cost of this gesture work — the carrying of material from one application to another — is the single largest hidden tax on a knowledge worker's day.

What has changed in the last twelve months is that the tools have learned, finally, to talk to each other directly. Claude can now read, with your permission, from the places your work already lives: Notion, Gmail, Drive, HubSpot, Linear, Asana, Slack, your calendar, your CRM, your support tickets, your accounting software. You do not paste. You do not export. You do not copy. You say what you want, and the material arrives.

The permission, importantly, is yours to give and yours to take back. It is granted per connector, per workspace, with a visible audit trail. It is the opposite of the old "give us read-write access to everything in your Google account" model that taught a generation of workers to be cautious about anything that asked. You connect what you need. You disconnect what you don't.

For an operator, the felt experience is this: the friction at the seam between applications — the friction that used to demand five minutes here, two minutes there, all day long — is simply gone. The folder is the folder. The inbox is the inbox. The brief gets written from real material, not from a hand-assembled copy of real material. And the ecosystem, as of this spring, covers more than six thousand business applications. The boring parts of the job have begun, finally, to stop being the job.

Three users, three quarters in

Three people. Three Tuesdays. Three hours of the week, recovered.

Names changed; the work is real. These are patterns we see again and again in CETI's training cohorts — not the first-day excitement, but what the work looks like nine months later, once the novelty has worn off and the habit has set in.

Marketing lead · Agency, twelve people

Proposals that used to take a day take an hour.

Maya kept a folder of past winning proposals the way some people keep a recipe box. Forty-eight documents, dating back four years, each one a slightly different shape of "yes." Before Claude, the folder was a reference she would dip into when she needed to remember how a clause was phrased. It was useful, but it was passive.

For the last nine months, the folder has been active. When a new RFP comes in, Maya gives Claude the brief and points it at the folder. She gets back a draft that already sounds like her firm — the cadence of the sections, the way her firm talks about pricing, the small turns of phrase her team is known for. She edits. She does not author from scratch. The proposal that used to take her a full Wednesday now takes ninety minutes. She is using that recovered time, mostly, to take on the kind of work she could not previously fit in.

“I'm not faster at writing. I am much, much faster at starting. That turns out to be most of it.”

Operations · DTC brand, $18M revenue

Vendor data, finally clean.

Diego inherited a supplier list that lived across six spreadsheets in six formats. Two of the suppliers were the same supplier under two different names. One had a quarterly price drift that nobody had caught. The list had been "on the list to clean up" for two and a half years — a project that was always one priority away.

In the first month of using Claude, Diego dropped all six spreadsheets into a Project. He asked for one reconciled view with cost trends by category, a flag on anything that looked like a duplicate, and a chart of price movement per supplier per quarter. The Artifact he got back was, in his words, "the version of this work I would have done if I had had three uninterrupted weeks." He now runs the same reconciliation as a monthly ritual. Last quarter, Claude caught a fourteen-per-cent price drift on a packaging supplier two weeks before it would have hit the P&L. That single catch paid for the year.

“The headache became a Monday-morning habit. That is the whole story.”

CEO · Services firm, forty people

Board updates that don't eat the weekend.

Priya used to spend Sunday mornings writing her monthly board update. Coffee, the kitchen table, the dread of starting. She would assemble the numbers, the wins, the misses, the "asks of the board." It took, on average, four hours. It took longer when the month had been hard.

The board update now starts on Friday afternoon, in Claude. Priya gives it the month's metrics, the team's weekly check-ins, and three talking points she wants to make. She gets back a draft already structured in the way her board likes to read — numbers first, then narrative, then asks. She rewrites the parts that need her voice, which is mostly the narrative and almost all of the asks. The draft is on her chairman's desk by Friday at six. Her Sunday mornings are her own again. The board, she reports, has not noticed a thing — except that the updates have become more consistent.

“It is not writing for me. It is getting me to the page. Once I am there, I am still me.”

A note in the margin

No. 03 · Sidebar

On using AI without losing your judgment.

There is a real worry, often unspoken in the room, that handing this kind of work to a model will atrophy the part of us that does the work. It is a worry worth taking seriously, and it is also — we have come to believe — the wrong framing.

Judgment is not built by doing the gathering. It is built by doing the deciding. The gathering — the searching, the formatting, the reconciling, the pasting — is not where a marketer becomes a better marketer or an operator becomes a better operator. Those skills are built in the moments when you weigh two options, when you choose what to leave out, when you tell a client a hard thing, when you sign your name to a number. None of that is being handed off here.

What is being handed off is the coordination tax. The work that used to fill the day around the work. And what we have observed, across the teams we train, is that when that tax is paid by something else, people do not become weaker. They become sharper, because they spend more of their time at the edge of the judgment they were hired for, and less time in the middle of administrative labour they had merely become accustomed to.

The rule we offer our clients is simple: let Claude do the gathering. Keep the deciding for yourself. The line is sometimes blurry, but the principle is not. You will know, in your stomach, when a decision is being made. Make it. Make it slowly. Make it well. That is the part that has your name on it.

— from the CETI cowork handbook, § 4.2

Three rates · May 2026

We are wary of the way pricing pages are usually written — in the breathless language of feature ladders and asterisked footnotes. The reality of Claude pricing in May 2026 is plainer than that, and we want to describe it plainly.

For an individual, Claude Pro is twenty dollars a month and includes everything we have written about in this issue: Opus, Sonnet, and Haiku models; Projects for grouping related work; Artifacts for the side-panel canvas; memory for continuity across conversations; and connectors to the apps your work already lives in. It is the tier we recommend to anyone who has not yet committed their team but who wants to see, for themselves, whether the change of frame is real.

For a team, Claude Team is twenty dollars per seat per month, billed annually, with a five-seat minimum. It adds shared Projects (the firm's collective memory, not just an individual's), centralized admin, a single bill, and the new Claude for Small Business workflows — fifteen pre-built patterns for common business work, launched in May 2026. We find this is the tier most operating teams settle on within their first month, because shared Projects turn out to be the unlock: when your colleagues' best prompts become part of the firm's working memory, the team's average output rises faster than any single member's would in isolation.

Three rates. Each is honest. Each has stopped being a reason to wait. The reason to wait, if there is one, is not the price; it is the absence of a frame for using the tool well. That is what this magazine is for, and that is what our workshop is for.
