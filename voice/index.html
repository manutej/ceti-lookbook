02Pre-Amp · The signal chain CH-02 / IN-LINE

The signal, end to end.

Speech in — meaning out — speech back. Five stages, one socket.

Capture User speaks VAD · 50 ms
Transcribe STT Whisper · 150 ms
Reason LLM GPT-4o · 400 ms
Synthesise TTS Eleven · 75 ms
Playback User hears Stream · 50 ms

A voice agent is fundamentally a signal chain — the same idea that runs through every analog studio rack ever built. Audio enters at the microphone, gets converted to text by a speech-to-text (STT) model, that text is consumed by a large language model (LLM) that decides what to say back, the response is rendered as audio by a text-to-speech (TTS) model, and the synthesised waveform is streamed to the listener. Five black boxes wired in series; the whole loop must close in under a second or the conversation breaks.

The 2026 reference stack typically pairs OpenAI Whisper Large V3 or Deepgram Nova-3 at the input (sub-150 ms partial transcripts, 70+ languages, word-level timestamps), a realtime-tuned LLM like gpt-4o-realtime or Gemini 3.1 Flash Live in the middle (200–400 ms time-to-first-token, tool-calling mid-stream), and an output stage of ElevenLabs Flash v3 or Cartesia Sonic-2 for synthesis (sub-75 ms first-chunk, voice cloning, emotional inflection). Every stage emits partial output before completion — that is the entire trick. Without streaming, you have a walkie-talkie; with streaming, you have a conversation.

The fifth stage — the one no provider sells — is the orchestration layer. It handles voice-activity detection (when does the user start? when do they stop?), barge-in (the user interrupts mid-utterance), turn-taking heuristics (how much silence counts as a turn?), and the network plumbing that holds the WebSocket open across the whole conversation. Frameworks like LiveKit Agents, Pipecat, Vapi, and Retell exist to wire the boxes together with sensible defaults; the OpenAI Realtime API folds the LLM and TTS into a single duplex socket so you skip a stage entirely. Pick your topology before you pick your models — the integration determines what's even possible.

The signal-chain metaphor is more than a teaching device. The same engineering instincts that produced great analog audio chains — clean staging, low noise floor, no clipping at any stage, headroom budgeted in advance — translate directly to voice-AI engineering. You design the loop end-to-end with a latency budget per stage. You instrument every boundary so you can see where the time and the quality are going. You build a bypass on every component so you can A/B-test a swap. And you treat the orchestrator as the master bus — the place where every stage's output gets summed, balanced, and shaped before reaching the listener. Engineers who came up wiring real racks tend to ship better voice agents than engineers who came up wiring REST APIs; the instincts transfer cleanly.

The number that matters in voice is 800 ms — the conversational ceiling above which humans unconsciously slow their cadence, over-explain, or start to repeat themselves. The unmistakable feeling of talking to a machine. Research on turn-taking in spoken dialogue (Stivers et al., across 10 languages) puts the human gap at 200 ms on average, with anything beyond 500 ms reading as hesitation. 800 ms is the polite outer edge; 1.2 s is phone-tree territory.

Where does the budget go? Capture takes ~50 ms (VAD has to actually decide you're done talking). STT emits its first token at 150 ms if you're streaming partials — you do not wait for the user to finish before transcription begins. LLM is the bottleneck, eating 300–500 ms of time-to-first-token; voice-tuned variants like gpt-4o-realtime trim this to ~200 ms. TTS contributes 75–150 ms of first-chunk synthesis. Network jitter steals another 50 ms on a clean connection, more if you're going carrier-PSTN over POTS interconnect.

The pipeline doesn't run in series at the LLM/TTS boundary — it pipelines. The TTS starts speaking word three while the LLM is still generating word twelve. The user starts hearing the response while the model is still composing it. Done well, the user perceives the agent as responsive even while the cold-start latency of each component is, individually, terrible. Get the streaming wrong and the budget triples overnight.

One subtler latency you don't see on most dashboards: perceptual latency — the gap between when the user finishes speaking and when they hear an acknowledgement, even a non-verbal one. A 600 ms gap with no filler feels worse than a 900 ms gap that opens with a short "mm-hmm" or "let me check." Pipecat and LiveKit both ship hooks for this; production agents that hit the perception bar usually layer an immediate non-content acknowledgement at 200 ms and follow it with the real response at 700 ms. The user perceives the latter as 200 ms because their conversational clock starts ticking at the acknowledgement, not at the final word of the response.

The STT layer is closer to a solved problem than the rest of the stack. Word error rates on clean English audio are within a percentage point of each other across the top tier — the differentiation is in streaming latency, language coverage, and the auxiliary features that matter for telephony: diarisation (who-spoke-when), profanity filtering, keyword boosting, and noise robustness on 8 kHz narrowband audio.

Deepgram Nova-3 is the industry default for call-centre and telephony work — sub-150 ms partial transcripts, strong diarisation, custom vocabulary boosting, and noise resilience tuned specifically for narrowband. Whisper Large V3 remains the open-source benchmark — ship it on your own GPUs and you pay the inference bill, but you keep the data; useful when your audio touches HIPAA, PII, or any policy that prohibits third-party processing. Cartesia Ink trades language breadth for sub-100 ms first tokens — the fastest streaming STT on the market, ideal when the LLM is your bottleneck and you need to claw back every millisecond at the input stage. AssemblyAI Universal-2 brings strong PII redaction and topic-detection auxiliary models that ship together with the transcript, useful for regulated industries; ElevenLabs Scribe v2 is the newcomer with the longest-tail language support, particularly strong on under-resourced African and South Asian languages where Whisper still struggles.

One practical detail that gets overlooked: streaming STT models emit both interim hypotheses (the running best guess, which keeps changing as more audio arrives) and finalised transcripts (the definitive text for a completed utterance). Your orchestrator needs to know which one to send to the LLM. Send only finals and you wait for the user to stop talking; send interims and the LLM thrashes on revisions. The right answer is usually to feed interims into a "should I start composing a response?" prediction and only commit the final to the LLM context window.

05Processor · LLMs for voice DSP-CORE

A "voice-good" LLM is not the same animal as a "long-form" LLM. The qualities that matter on a phone call are: low time-to-first-token (so the user hears the agent start to answer fast), instruction-following on short fragments (because partial STT output arrives as the user is still talking), terse default responses (because nobody wants a four-paragraph answer spoken at 180 wpm), and tool-calling support mid-stream (because the agent needs to look up your calendar without first finishing its sentence).

The native-realtime models — gpt-4o-realtime and Gemini 3.1 Flash Live — collapse the LLM and TTS into a single duplex audio socket. You send audio in, the model sends audio back, and the boundary between "reasoning" and "speaking" disappears from your wire format. This buys you 200–400 ms of avoided round-trips but locks you into a single vendor for both stages. For agents that need to pick a custom voice, multilingual switching mid-utterance, or per-tenant voice cloning, the decoupled stack (text-LLM + standalone TTS) is still the right call. Groq-hosted Llama 3.3 70B is the budget pick — sub-second loop latency at one-twentieth the price — but you'll write more system-prompt-engineering to keep it terse.

Prompt design for voice is its own discipline. The system prompt should explicitly cap response length ("answer in one or two sentences unless asked for detail"), forbid markdown ("never use lists, headings, or asterisks — this will be spoken aloud"), and instruct on conversational pacing ("if the user interrupts, stop immediately and listen"). Voice agents that escape from a text-mode prompt tend to produce three-paragraph monologues that crater the perceived responsiveness even when the technical latency is fine. The model is doing what you asked; you just asked the wrong thing.

TTS is where the agent stops sounding like a robot. The metrics that matter are Mean Opinion Score (MOS — the 1-to-5 naturalness rating from blind listening tests), first-byte latency (how fast the first audio chunk arrives), and voice-cloning fidelity (can you spin up a new voice from a 30-second sample?).

ElevenLabs Flash v3 is the current top of the heap on naturalness — MOS 4.6 in independent blind tests, emotional inflection from inline [laughs] and [whispers] tags, 380 voices across 70+ languages, mid-utterance language switching. Cartesia Sonic-2 is the speed champion — sub-50 ms TTFB via a state-space-model architecture that gives up a sliver of emotional range for raw throughput. OpenAI Voice is the safe default if you're already on Realtime API — nine stock voices, no cloning, but the integration is one line. Resemble leads on voice cloning — production-grade clones from three-second samples — for use cases that need a specific person's voice (audiobook narration, branded virtual assistants, accessibility tools that preserve a user's own voice after voice loss).

Two non-obvious axes also matter. First, SSML / control-tag support: can you make the voice pause, emphasise, whisper, or laugh on demand? Eleven's bracket-tag syntax is the most expressive; OpenAI Voice exposes very little control. Second, stability vs. variability: TTS models trade off how close each utterance sticks to the canonical voice (stable = predictable, good for IVR; variable = more human, good for companion agents). Eleven exposes both as tunable parameters; Cartesia ships sensible defaults and hides the knobs. Pick stability for transactional flows and variability for relational ones.

Low-code competitor to Vapi. Pre-built flows for lead qualification, support triage, appointment booking, and follow-ups. Deploy a working agent in minutes; less control than Vapi at the wire level but faster to ship.

First-party · bundled

OpenAI Realtime API

No orchestrator. The Realtime API is the orchestrator — you open one WebSocket, send audio, receive audio. Tool-calling, interruption, function-calling all bundled in. The lowest-friction way to ship an agent; locks you to OpenAI for the full stack.

Pricing: $0.06/min audio in · $0.24/min audio out · 300–500 ms loop

The orchestration layer is where production voice gets hard. It is the only stage in the signal chain that is concurrent by design — every other stage has a clear input and output, but the orchestrator has to listen and talk at the same time, decide when the user has actually stopped speaking (not just paused), interrupt itself cleanly when the user barges in, manage the WebSocket reconnection if the network blips, dispatch tool calls without breaking audio flow, and emit clean telemetry so you can debug a six-second silence at 2 AM.

The choice between hosted (Vapi, Retell, Bland) and framework (Pipecat, LiveKit) is a make-vs-buy decision on those concurrency primitives. The hosted platforms ship with reasonable defaults and a control panel; the frameworks give you the wires and a soldering iron. The OpenAI Realtime API short-circuits the question entirely — if you're happy locked to OpenAI for STT + LLM + TTS, you don't need an orchestrator at all. Most production deployments end up with either Vapi (when shipping fast matters) or LiveKit Agents + Pipecat (when control matters).

08Walkthrough · End-to-end build PATCH-BAY / WIRING

Twelve lines, one phone call.

A working voice agent on the OpenAI Realtime API — from socket to speaker.

voice-agent.ts TypeScript · Node 20+ 
import WebSocket from "ws";

const ws = new WebSocket(
  "wss://api.openai.com/v1/realtime?model=gpt-4o-realtime",
  { headers: { Authorization: `Bearer ${process.env.OPENAI_API_KEY}` } }
);

ws.on("open", () => ws.send(JSON.stringify({
  type: "session.update",
  session: {
    voice: "verse",
    modalities: ["audio", "text"],
    turn_detection: { type: "server_vad", threshold: 0.5 },
    instructions: "You are a terse, helpful phone assistant."
  }
})));

ws.on("message", (buf) => {
  const e = JSON.parse(buf.toString());
  if (e.type === "response.audio.delta") play(e.delta); // stream to speaker
});

What each parameter does: voice: "verse" selects one of nine stock voices (verse, alloy, echo, fable, onyx, nova, sage, shimmer, coral). modalities tells the model to emit both audio chunks and a text transcript on the same socket. turn_detection: server_vad lets OpenAI's server-side voice-activity detector decide when the user has stopped talking; threshold: 0.5 is the silence-confidence cutoff (lower = more eager to interrupt, higher = waits for clearer pauses). instructions is your system prompt — keep it terse, because the model is going to speak what it generates.

The shape of the API is the lesson. One bidirectional WebSocket carries everything: your microphone capture (as base64-encoded PCM16 audio deltas you push into the socket), control events (session updates, tool-call responses, interruption signals), and the model's response (audio deltas you decode and play, plus text deltas you can log or display). There is no separate STT endpoint, no separate TTS endpoint, no orchestration glue between them — the Realtime API is the orchestrator. The same is true of Google's Gemini Live API and (with different protocols) every native-realtime offering.

For a production agent you'd add: a Twilio Media Stream WebSocket on the other end (so the same code answers a real phone number); a tool-call handler (so the agent can look up your calendar, send an email, or hand off to a human); an interruption handler (clear the playback buffer when the user barges in mid-sentence); a fallback recipe (if the WebSocket drops, replay the conversation summary into a fresh session and keep going). Twenty-five minutes of additional code; the architecture stays exactly this simple.

09Troubleshooting · Common faults DIAGNOSTIC

When the signal goes wrong.

Five faults you will hit in your first week of production traffic.

VAD false positive. The agent thinks the user finished speaking when they were just mid-breath, and interrupts itself or replies to a fragment. Raise VAD threshold · add 200 ms silence padding
Echo / acoustic feedback. The agent's own TTS output gets picked up by the mic and re-transcribed, so it ends up "responding" to itself in a loop. Enable echo-cancellation · mute mic during TTS playback
Hallucinated speech. STT produces fluent transcripts of pure background noise, ambient music, or silence — common with Whisper on long-silence segments. Add no-speech-probability filter · gate on VAD activity
Wake-word ghost. The model thinks it heard a wake word ("Hey, agent") in the middle of a normal turn and starts a new conversation. Disable wake-word detection mid-session · use explicit handoff state
Bargein-loop deadlock. User interrupts agent mid-sentence; agent stops, asks "sorry?"; user responds; agent starts again from the top — same words, same interruption, repeat forever. Track interruption count · switch to terse-mode after 2 in a row

Almost every fault in this list traces back to the boundary between the voice-activity-detector and the language model. VAD is a binary signal — speaking or not — trying to gate a fundamentally analog phenomenon. The model downstream has no understanding of acoustic context; it just receives "here is a transcript, respond." Treat the VAD output as a probabilistic recommendation rather than ground truth: log both the silence-confidence score and the no-speech-probability, and build dashboards on the failure modes so you can tune thresholds per-deployment rather than per-call.

10Applications · What it's good for USE-CASE / IN-FIELD

Where voice already works.

Five categories shipping production traffic at scale today.

L1 triage, appointment booking, billing inquiries, status checks — the bread-and-butter of the field.

CH-02
Accessibility

Screen-reader-replacement, voice-first OS shells, real-time captioning for the hard-of-hearing.

CH-03
Voice notes

Dictation + summarisation. Doctors transcribing patient notes, lawyers drafting on the commute.

CH-04
Live translation

Sub-second cross-language voice with the original speaker's vocal timbre preserved.

CH-05
Ambient assistants

Always-listening agents on headphones, smart-speakers, in-car systems. The post-Alexa generation.

The economic centre of voice today is outbound and inbound telephony. Vapi and Retell together handle several million calls per day for sales outreach, customer support, appointment confirmation, and back-office workflows that used to require a human picking up a phone. The unit economics are stark: a human call-centre agent in the US costs $0.50–$1.00 per minute fully loaded; a voice-AI agent costs $0.15–$0.40 per minute including the LLM, TTS, STT, and the orchestrator's margin. The replacement happens fastest in domains where the conversation has a known shape — lead qualification, "is your order ready?", appointment booking — and slowest in domains that require empathic judgement or open-ended discovery.

Outside telephony, the next wave is ambient assistants: agents living in headphones (Meta Ray-Bans, AirPods with hearables), smart-speakers, and cars, listening continuously and surfacing context just-in-time. Real-time translation is the killer demo — the original speaker's voice, preserved in timbre and emotion, rendered into a different language within a half-second — and the technology is essentially there as of late 2026. Accessibility remains the most under-served use case relative to its impact: voice-first OS shells, real-time captioning, screen-reader replacement. Voice is no longer a curiosity. It's a primitive.
