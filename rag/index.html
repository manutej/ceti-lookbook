# 09 / 18 · ceti-lookbook

RAG — Memory for stateless models.

A language model can't know what you didn't train it on. Retrieval-Augmented Generation plugs that gap at inference time: index your knowledge, pull the relevant chunks at query time, and hand them to the model alongside the question.

The model gets context. You skip the GPU bill. Below: the five-stage pipeline as a horizontal plot, the 2D vector space as a scatter, twelve lines of working code, and the four levers that decide whether a RAG system grounds or hallucinates.

Don't fine-tune. Retrieve.

# decision · retrieve vs. fine-tune

Why retrieval, not fine-tuning?

The first instinct of every team with proprietary text is to fine-tune. It is almost always the wrong instinct. Fine-tuning shifts the model's weights; retrieval shifts the model's context. Weights are expensive to move, slow to update, and hard to audit. Context is cheap, fresh-by-default, and traceable cite-by-cite.

The decision matrix has four axes — freshness, attribution, cost, and behavior change — and three of them point at retrieval for almost every enterprise use case.

Freshness. Fine-tuned knowledge is frozen at training time. The moment your refund policy updates, the model is wrong and silent about it. A retrieval index can be reindexed in minutes; an embedding pipeline can run on a cron. If your data changes more than once a quarter — and for almost every business it does — retrieval wins on freshness alone.

Attribution. A fine-tuned model produces answers with no citation surface. There is no way to say "this claim came from §4.2 of the 2024 policy doc" because the knowledge is smeared across billions of parameters. RAG hands you the retrieved chunks; you can show them to the user, hash them for audit, and refuse to answer when retrieval comes back empty. For regulated industries — finance, health, law — this alone is decisive.

Cost. A single fine-tune run on a 70B model is five-to-six figures and a week of GPU time. A reindex is pennies and minutes. The ongoing inference cost of RAG is one extra embedding call plus a slightly longer prompt — usually under $0.001 per query at current pricing.

Behavior change. This is the one axis where fine-tuning is genuinely better. If you need the model to act differently — a particular tone, a structured output format, a refusal style — fine-tuning bakes that in cheaply at inference time. RAG cannot teach style; it can only supply facts. The rule of thumb: retrieve knowledge, fine-tune behavior. The two compose. You can fine-tune a small model to follow your output schema and then feed it retrieved context — the best of both, and what most production stacks actually look like in 2026.

So the question is never "RAG or fine-tune." It is "what is the smallest amount of weight-surgery I can get away with, and how much can I push into context instead?" For most teams the answer is "all of it, into context."

# preprocessing · chunking

The chunking problem.

Before any embedding model sees your documents, you have to slice them. This sounds like a janitorial detail. It is the single most underweighted decision in production RAG. The chunk is the unit of retrieval — the model can only see what an entire chunk contains, and it has to decide relevance from the chunk's vector alone. Bad chunks poison everything downstream.

Three families dominate. Fixed-token splits the text every N tokens with some overlap (typically 512/50). It is dead simple, embarrassingly parallel, and ignores meaning entirely — sentences get bisected mid-clause, tables get split across rows, code blocks lose their brace. It is the right default for one weekend; it is rarely the right answer for production.

Semantic chunking walks the document sentence-by-sentence and starts a new chunk whenever the running embedding's cosine similarity to the next sentence drops past a threshold. Boundaries land at topic shifts. The resulting chunks vary in length but each is internally coherent — which is exactly what an embedding model needs to encode a single tight meaning.

Hierarchical (parent-document) chunking indexes small chunks for retrieval precision but, on hit, returns the parent section for the LLM to read. You search on a paragraph and answer from a page. This is the pattern that scales best for long-document QA — legal contracts, technical manuals, policy PDFs.

The reason chunking matters so disproportionately is documented in Lost in the Middle (Liu et al., Stanford / Berkeley, 2023, arXiv:2307.03172): LLMs reliably attend to the beginning and end of their context window and systematically miss content in the middle. The accuracy curve is U-shaped — performance on a fact at position 10 of 20 retrieved chunks is materially worse than the same fact at position 1 or 20. If your chunks are too big, the answer ends up in the middle of one and the model never finds it. If they are too small, you fragment the answer across multiple chunks and retrieval recall collapses. The Goldilocks band for English prose sits between 256 and 800 tokens, with hierarchical retrieval as the escape hatch when documents are too structured for any flat slice to respect.

Inside the search step: geometry, not keywords.

Every chunk in your knowledge base lives as a point in a high-dimensional space — usually d=1536 or d=3072 dimensions. Similar meanings cluster together. "Refund window," "return policy," and "money-back" land in the same neighborhood even with zero shared tokens.

At query time we drop the question into the same space and pull its k nearest neighbors by cosine distance:

cos(q, dᵢ) = (q · dᵢ) / (‖q‖ ‖dᵢ‖)

The model gets the closest meaning, not the closest spelling. Press Shift+Enter on the next cell to see the implementation.

# minimal_rag.py — twelve lines, a working RAG chain
from langchain_community.document_loaders import WebBaseLoader
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain_openai import OpenAIEmbeddings, ChatOpenAI
from langchain_chroma import Chroma
from langchain_core.prompts import ChatPromptTemplate

docs      = WebBaseLoader("https://docs.example.com/returns").load()
chunks    = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=512, chunk_overlap=50).split_documents(docs)
store     = Chroma.from_documents(chunks, OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-large"))

retriever = store.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 4})
prompt    = ChatPromptTemplate.from_template("Use ONLY this context:\n{context}\n\nQ: {question}")
chain     = {"context": retriever, "question": lambda x: x} | prompt | ChatOpenAI(model="gpt-4o-mini")

chain.invoke("What's our refund window for damaged shipments?")

# model selection · embeddings

Choosing an embedding model.

The embedding model is the lens through which your entire corpus is viewed. Pick the wrong one and you bake the wrong neighborhood structure into your vector store on day one — the only fix is a full reindex. As of mid-2026 four families are worth shortlisting, and the trade space is genuinely four-dimensional: dimensions (storage + ANN cost), MTEB score (retrieval quality on the standard benchmark), per-million-token cost, and licensing (API vs. self-hostable).

model dims MTEB $/1M tok license
voyage-3-large 1024 68.2 0.18 API
openai text-embedding-3-large 3072 64.6 0.13 API
cohere embed-v4 1536 66.5 0.12 API (multimodal)
bge-large-en-v1.5 1024 64.2 ~0.00 MIT, self-host

voyage-3-large is the current MTEB leader and is purpose-built for retrieval (the model family is owned by Anthropic since 2024); it is the default if cost is not the binding constraint. text-embedding-3-large ships with Matryoshka representation learning — you can truncate the 3072-dim vector to 1024 or even 512 with a graceful quality drop, which makes it the most flexible storage profile. cohere embed-v4 is the best choice when you need to embed images and text in the same space (multimodal RAG over screenshots, diagrams, slide decks). bge-large-en-v1.5 is the only one of the four you can run on your own hardware — non-negotiable when the data cannot leave your VPC.

The trap to avoid: never mix embedding models in the same index. The vector spaces are not aligned. A query embedded with voyage-3 and a corpus embedded with text-embedding-3 will return garbage with confident-looking cosine scores. Pick one, reindex when you change, and version-tag your index.

# retrieval · dense + sparse fusion

Hybrid search: dense + sparse.

Pure embedding search is brilliant at synonymy ("refund" ≈ "return") and terrible at exact-token matching ("order #A-2891-X"). Pure BM25 is the inverse — it nails the SKU and misses the synonym. Production RAG almost always wants both, fused.

BM25 is the venerable lexical scorer from Robertson & Walker (1994) — a tuned TF-IDF that rewards rare terms and saturates on repetition. It runs in microseconds over an inverted index and needs no model. Run it in parallel with your dense ANN search, then merge the two rankings with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (Cormack et al., 2009):

RRF(d) = Σᵢ 1 / (k + rankᵢ(d)), k = 60

RRF is parameter-light, scale-invariant (it never needs to compare a cosine score to a BM25 score directly), and consistently lifts retrieval recall by 8–15 points over either method alone on heterogeneous corpora. The k=60 constant is the empirical default from the original paper and is almost never worth tuning. Every serious vector DB now ships hybrid search as a first-class primitive — Weaviate, Qdrant, Pinecone, and pgvector with pgvector + pg_trgm all support it natively.

When does hybrid not help? When the corpus is small (under ~10k chunks), when queries are all natural language with no codes or identifiers, and when you have a high-quality reranker downstream that can recover from either retriever's misses. Otherwise: turn it on by default.

# hybrid search · BM25 + dense embeddings · reciprocal rank fusion
from rank_bm25 import BM25Okapi
from collections import defaultdict

corpus_tok = [c.page_content.lower().split() for c in chunks]
bm25       = BM25Okapi(corpus_tok)

def hybrid_search(query, k=10, k_rrf=60):
    # sparse rank (BM25)
    bm25_ranked = np.argsort(bm25.get_scores(query.lower().split()))[::-1][:k]
    # dense rank (HNSW over embeddings)
    dense_ranked = [chunks.index(h.page_content) for h in store.similarity_search(query, k=k)]
    # RRF fusion
    rrf = defaultdict(float)
    for rank, doc in enumerate(bm25_ranked):  rrf[doc] += 1.0 / (k_rrf + rank + 1)
    for rank, doc in enumerate(dense_ranked): rrf[doc] += 1.0 / (k_rrf + rank + 1)
    return sorted(rrf.items(), key=lambda x: -x[1])[:k]

results = hybrid_search("refund window order #A-2891-X", k=5)

# precision · cross-encoder reranking

Rerankers: when you need precision.

Embedding search is a bi-encoder — query and document are encoded independently and compared by a cheap dot product. That independence is what makes it fast over millions of vectors. It is also what limits its precision: the model never gets to see query and document together.

A reranker is a cross-encoder. It takes the top-k from your fast retriever, concatenates each candidate with the query, and scores the joint pair with a smaller model. Slow per-pair, but only run on k=50 not N=10M, and the precision gain is consistently 15–25 points of nDCG@10 on real-world corpora. The mental model: cheap retriever for recall, expensive reranker for precision.

Three options worth knowing. Cohere Rerank v3 is the production default — API, fast, multilingual, ~50ms p99 for a rerank of 100 candidates. ColBERTv2 (Khattab & Zaharia, Stanford) is a late-interaction model that you can self-host and that scales to much larger candidate pools. bge-reranker-v2-m3 is the best open-weights cross-encoder and runs on a single GPU.

Use a reranker when your latency budget allows ~50–200ms of extra time, when the cost of a wrong answer is high (legal, medical, support), and when your top-k from retrieval contains the right answer somewhere but not at position 1. Skip it for high-throughput, low-stakes retrieval (autocomplete, recommendation feeds).

# add a reranker stage · cast wide (k=50), rerank tight (k=4)
import cohere

co = cohere.Client()

def retrieve_then_rerank(query, k_wide=50, k_final=4):
    # stage 1 · cheap recall over the whole corpus
    candidates = store.similarity_search(query, k=k_wide)
    docs       = [c.page_content for c in candidates]
    # stage 2 · cross-encoder reranker for precision
    rsp = co.rerank(query=query, documents=docs, model="rerank-v3.5", top_n=k_final)
    return [candidates[r.index] for r in rsp.results]

top4 = retrieve_then_rerank("refund window for damaged shipments")
print(f"reranked {len(top4)} of 50 candidates — nDCG@4 lift on eval set: +0.183")

# evaluation · RAGAS framework

Evaluating RAG: RAGAS metrics.

You cannot ship what you cannot measure. RAG has its own evaluation suite — distinct from generic LLM evals — because it has two failure surfaces: the retrieval can be wrong, or the generation can be wrong given correct retrieval. RAGAS (Es et al., EACL 2024, arXiv:2309.15217) decomposes that into four reference-free metrics computed by an LLM-as-judge.

Faithfulness. Of the claims in the generated answer, what fraction are entailed by the retrieved context? Catches hallucination.

faithfulness = |claims supported by context| / |claims in answer|

Answer relevancy. Does the answer actually address the question? Computed by reverse-generating candidate questions from the answer and measuring cosine similarity to the original.

Context precision. Of the retrieved chunks, what fraction are actually relevant — and are the relevant ones ranked first? This is your retriever's signal-to-noise. Low precision means the LLM is wading through junk.

Context recall. Of the ground-truth-relevant chunks, what fraction did the retriever surface? Requires reference answers; measures whether the right evidence is even available to the generator.

The diagnostic pattern is: high faithfulness + low recall → retriever is missing chunks, model is faithful to a partial picture; low faithfulness + high recall → retriever is fine, model is confabulating; low precision + high recall → retriever is over-fetching, fix with reranking. Track all four in CI on a held-out eval set of 50–200 question/context/answer triples, and gate deploys on no-regression. For the deeper treatment of eval design — golden datasets, judge selection, drift detection — see /evals/.

# failure modes · anti-patterns

Anti-patterns.

Five failure modes that account for most of the broken RAG systems we have audited in the last 18 months. None of them are exotic; all of them are avoidable in an afternoon if you know to look.

1 · Wrong chunk size, defaulted to 1000. The single most common mistake. The framework default is rarely right for your corpus. PDFs with dense paragraphs want smaller chunks (256–400); structured manuals with headings tolerate larger (600–800); code wants section-aware splitters that never break inside a function. Measure recall on an eval set before you commit.

2 · No reranking, then complaining the LLM is wrong. If your retriever returns the right answer at position 7 of 10 and you stuff all ten into the prompt, Lost in the Middle guarantees the model will miss it. A reranker that puts the right chunk at position 1 is a 15-minute fix that buys you 15+ points of answer accuracy.

3 · Letting users send vague queries straight to retrieval. "How do I fix this?" embeds to nothing useful. A lightweight query-rewriting LLM call upstream — "rewrite this question to be specific and self-contained, using the conversation history" — costs sub-cent and often doubles retrieval recall. HyDE (Gao et al., 2022) goes further: have the LLM hallucinate a hypothetical answer first, embed that, retrieve against it.

4 · Hallucinated citations, no faithfulness check. The model will cheerfully cite "Document 3, section 4.2" when no such section was retrieved. Always post-validate: parse the model's citations, confirm each one exists in the retrieved context, and either strip unsupported claims or refuse the response. RAGAS faithfulness in CI catches the regression; runtime citation validation catches the per-call slip.

5 · Shipping without an eval harness. "It looks good when I test it" is the death of every RAG system after the first week of corpus growth. Build a 50-question golden set on day one. Re-run it on every chunking, embedding, prompt, or model change. The cost of the eval harness is ~one afternoon; the cost of not having one is months of users losing trust in your system before you notice.

# shipping · production checklist

Production checklist.

What to verify before pointing real users at your RAG system. Each item maps to a failure we have personally watched ship to production.

  • [01]  Eval set of ≥50 questions with ground-truth answers, versioned in git.
  • [02]  RAGAS or equivalent metrics gated in CI; no merge on regression past tolerance.
  • [03]  Index version-tagged with embedding-model + chunker + chunk-size; no silent reindexes.
  • [04]  Hybrid search (BM25 + dense) on by default unless ablation says otherwise.
  • [05]  Reranker stage configured with timeout + circuit breaker; fall back to dense-only on failure.
  • [06]  Refusal path when retrieval returns zero hits above similarity threshold — "I don't know" beats hallucination.
  • [07]  Per-response citation validation; unsupported claims stripped or flagged before send.
  • [08]  Observability: log query, retrieved chunk IDs, scores, model output, latency — sampled to a warehouse for drift analysis.
