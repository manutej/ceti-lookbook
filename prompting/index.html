§ 11.0 · Preface

11.0 Why prompt patterns matter

A large language model is, mechanically, a function from a sequence of tokens to a probability distribution over the next token. The text you place in front of it is therefore not commentary on the program — it is the program. This sounds like a slogan, and at first it feels like one, but the implications run deeper than newcomers usually expect. Programs that are made of prose have all the problems prose has: ambiguity, register, audience assumption, and the slow drift of meaning under copy-and-paste. The way to keep a prose program from rotting is the same way a software program is kept from rotting: structure, conventions, and a small library of reusable solutions to recurring problems.

This chapter assembles that small library. It is deliberately not exhaustive. The six patterns presented here — Chain-of-Thought (§11.1), Few-Shot (§11.2), Role Prompting (§11.3), Structured Output (§11.4), Self-Consistency (§11.5), and ReAct (§11.6) — cover roughly ninety percent of the prompt-engineering work CETI sees inside enterprise teams from 2023 through early 2026. They are the design patterns of the Gang-of-Four book, ported to a different programming substrate. Like those patterns, their value lies less in any single one of them than in the shared vocabulary they give a team. Once a colleague can say “we have a Few-Shot drift problem; let’s add Structured Output and pin a Role,” the conversation moves at the pace of design rather than the pace of guesswork.

The treatment for each pattern is uniform. We give a formal definition that does not mistake the pattern for one of its accidental implementations; we trace the academic provenance briefly so the reader can find the seminal paper; we state when the pattern earns its weight, and — this matters — when it does not. Every pattern has an anti-pattern: a circumstance in which applying it actively makes the model worse. We then show two code listings, a basic and an advanced, both runnable against the Anthropic Messages API or any compatible provider. A “gotcha” paragraph captures the failure mode we see most often in production. Finally we cite at least one empirical study or benchmark so the reader can verify the lift number rather than take our word for it.

A production prompt almost never uses one pattern in isolation. §11.8 demonstrates a five-pattern composition that classifies and routes inbound customer email; the chapter closes with a comparative table (§11.7), a list of common mistakes (§11.9), and an index that cross-references this chapter to the rest of the handbook — in particular to Ch. 14 (LangGraph) and Ch. 19 (MCP), where these patterns reappear inside agentic systems and tool ecosystems respectively.

A note on terminology. We use “prompt” to mean the full input passed to the model on a given turn — system message, conversation history, retrieved context, examples, and user query, treated as one artifact. The terms “prompt” and “context window” are not synonyms: the context window is the substrate, the prompt is what you choose to put in it.

§ 11.1 · Reasoning patterns

11.1

Chain-of-Thought Aliases: CoT · “Let’s think step by step” · scratchpad prompting

See also §11.5 Self-Consistency (samples multiple CoT traces) · §11.6 ReAct (interleaves CoT with tool calls) · Ch. 14 LangGraph — Reasoning Nodes

Chain-of-Thought is the practice of instructing a language model to externalise its intermediate reasoning before committing to a final answer. The instruction can be as direct as appending the literal sentence “Let’s think step by step” to the prompt, or as elaborate as wrapping the reasoning inside reserved XML tags such as <thinking>…</thinking> that the application layer later strips before showing the user the final answer. Either way the mechanic is the same: by allocating tokens to intermediate steps, the model performs more computation per inference, and the steps themselves become available for inspection.

The pattern entered the literature with Wei et al. (2022), who demonstrated that supplying just a handful of worked reasoning examples lifted GSM8K accuracy on the 540-billion-parameter PaLM model from roughly 18 percent to roughly 57 percent — a jump of nearly forty points achieved without changing the model weights at all. Kojima et al. (2022) later showed that even the bare zero-shot trigger phrase produced most of the lift on sufficiently large models. Since then the pattern has been absorbed into model training itself: contemporary frontier models, including Claude 4 and GPT-4.1, have a tendency to produce chain-of-thought-like reasoning by default. The pattern remains essential nonetheless, because explicit instruction concentrates the reasoning into a controllable region of the output.

When to use it

Reach for Chain-of-Thought whenever the task requires multiple inferential steps that depend on each other: word problems, multi-hop questions, logical puzzles, code review with cause-and-effect tracing, financial calculations, and any task where the model must compare alternatives. The signature symptom is that the model returns plausible-sounding but inconsistent answers when run several times: it is guessing at the leaves of a tree it has not built.

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

Three failure modes are worth naming. First, for tasks that are purely retrievals — “what is the capital of Burkina Faso” — Chain-of-Thought adds latency and cost without changing the answer. Second, for short-form classification tasks where the label space is small and the decision is mostly perceptual, the reasoning can drift and the model may talk itself out of a correct first instinct. Third, on small models (under roughly seven billion parameters in 2026), Wei et al. observed that CoT can hurt accuracy: the model is large enough to articulate reasoning but not large enough to execute it correctly, and the resulting confidently-wrong scratchpad poisons the final answer. The rule of thumb: use CoT for hard reasoning on capable models, skip it for shallow retrieval on any model.

+39 points GSM8K accuracy lift · PaLM-540B · Wei et al. 2022

Listing 11.1 — basic CoT trigger

11.1Basic zero-shot Chain-of-Thought — one line, no examples.

# Zero-shot CoT — the single most effective
# prompt-engineering line of the decade.
prompt = """
Q: A bookstore has 14 shelves, each holding 23 books.
   They sell 47 books on Monday. How many remain?
A: Let's think step by step.
"""

response = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-7",
    max_tokens=512,
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}]
)

Listing 11.2 — structured CoT with tag isolation

11.2Advanced: tag-isolated reasoning the application can strip. — Anthropic-style.

system = """
You are a financial analyst. For every question:
  1. Reason inside <thinking>...</thinking> tags.
  2. Emit ONLY the final answer in <answer> tags.
  3. The reasoning may be long. The answer must be terse.
"""

# downstream code keeps only the <answer> payload
import re
match = re.search(r"<answer>(.*?)</answer>",
                  response.content[0].text,
                  re.DOTALL)
final = match.group(1).strip() if match else ""
Gotcha The reasoning a model emits is not always the reasoning it used. Chain-of-Thought traces are post-hoc rationalisations the model is willing to commit to — they are an audit trail of the answer, not a window into the network. Treat them as you would a deposition: useful, persuasive, sometimes wrong. For the underlying empirical worry, see Turpin et al. (2023) on biased reasoning in CoT.

§ 11.2 · Demonstration patterns

11.2

Few-Shot Prompting Aliases: in-context learning · k-shot · demonstration prompting

See also §11.4 Structured Output (examples teach the schema) · Ch. 7 RAG — passage formatting

Few-Shot prompting supplies the model with a small number of worked input–output pairs immediately before the actual input, and asks the model to continue the implied pattern. The technique was a centrepiece of the GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020), which demonstrated that large autoregressive models could perform reliably on novel tasks after seeing only two to thirty-two demonstrations — no fine-tuning required. The paper called the underlying behaviour in-context learning, and that name has since become the more general term: Few-Shot is the most common implementation of in-context learning, but not its only one.

The mechanism is best understood by analogy. Without examples, the model must infer from the task description alone what shape the answer should take. With three or five worked examples, the model has both the task description and a concrete demonstration of the desired register, format, edge-case handling, and level of verbosity. The examples constitute a kind of micro-fine-tune that lives entirely in the context window and disappears when the conversation ends.

When to use it

Few-Shot earns its tokens whenever the desired output shape is hard to specify in prose but easy to demonstrate: classifying sentiment into a custom taxonomy, translating between a source and a domain-specific target dialect, generating SQL in a particular team style, rewriting customer complaints in a brand voice. The pattern is also the cleanest way to teach format: three JSON examples beat three paragraphs of schema description every time.

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

Two failure modes recur. First, padding the prompt with too many examples. Brown et al. observed diminishing returns past roughly eight demonstrations on most tasks, and we observe in client work that beyond ten examples the model starts pattern-matching on the examples themselves rather than on the underlying task — producing outputs that are eerily similar to one of the demonstrations regardless of the actual input. Second, using examples that are too narrow: three sentiment examples that all happen to be product reviews will train the model to expect product reviews, and it will misclassify a tweet. The fix is variety, not volume.

3 — 5 examples Sweet spot on most tasks · Brown et al. 2020 · Liu et al. 2022

Listing 11.4 — XML-tagged Few-Shot with reasoning

11.4Tag-fenced demonstrations — safer for production. — Composes with §11.1.

system = """
Extract the meeting time from each message.

<example>
  <input>Let's sync tomorrow at 3pm PT</input>
  <reasoning>"tomorrow" = next day; 3pm PT = 15:00 PST</reasoning>
  <output>{"time": "15:00", "tz": "PST", "day": "+1"}</output>
</example>

<example>
  <input>Coffee Thursday morning?</input>
  <reasoning>Day named, no time → default to 09:00 local</reasoning>
  <output>{"time": "09:00", "tz": "local", "day": "Thu"}</output>
</example>
"""
Gotcha The order of your examples matters more than newcomers expect. Lu et al. (2022) showed that simply re-ordering four demonstrations could swing accuracy by more than ten percentage points on the same task and the same model. The safe default: place the example whose format and edge-case handling most resembles the typical user input last. The model weighs recency.

§ 11.3 · Voicing patterns

11.3

Role Prompting Aliases: persona prompting · system framing · the “You are” pattern

See also §11.4 Structured Output (role + schema = brand-safe output) · Ch. 9 Safety — system prompts as guardrails

Role Prompting sets a persona, audience, and stakes for the model before it sees the actual user request. Implemented as a one-sentence system message — "You are a senior tax attorney advising a small-business owner who has never filed quarterly estimates before" — the role prefix simultaneously selects a vocabulary, an assumed shared knowledge base, a register, and a tone. In a single line it does work that would otherwise require several paragraphs of instruction, and it does so in a region of the prompt that most providers weight heavily.

The pattern is older than the transformer era; salespeople have used it for decades under the name “framing.” Within LLM practice it was popularised by White et al. (2023) in their “Prompt Pattern Catalog” and was systematically studied by Salewski et al. (2023), who found that even loosely-specified expert personas produced measurably better reasoning on domain-specific tasks — though crucially not always reliably so. The honest report from the 2024–2026 literature is that role prompting is a real and useful lever, but a noisy one: it changes outputs reliably, it improves outputs probabilistically.

When to use it

Use Role Prompting whenever the audience, register, or domain of the desired answer is non-obvious from the user’s question alone. A user who asks “what is entropy?” expects a different answer from a kindergarten teacher than from a senior physicist; the model has no way to know which the user wants unless the application layer tells it. Role prompts are particularly effective for safety alignment (“You are a helpful, harmless assistant who refuses requests for medical diagnosis”), brand voice enforcement (“You are the friendly support voice of Acme, never sarcastic, never apologetic”), and code-style consistency (“You are a strict reviewer who flags any function longer than 30 lines”).

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

Avoid roles that imply credentials the user might reasonably trust — “You are a licensed physician” or “You are a securities lawyer.” The model is none of these things, and downstream users may take the output more seriously than is warranted. A better pattern is to name the register the role implies (“Explain in the technical register a securities lawyer would use, but do not give legal advice”) rather than the credentialed identity itself. Also avoid stacking incompatible roles: a single prompt cannot reliably be both “a warm friend” and “a strict auditor” — the model will mix them and the result will be off in both directions.

11.5Single-sentence role — the most common form. — Goes in the system slot.

Listing 11.6 — layered role with explicit guardrails

11.6Role plus constraints plus refusal policy. — Production pattern.

system = """
ROLE: You are the customer-support voice of Northwind Bank.
REGISTER: Calm, factual, never apologetic-by-default, never
         flowery. Maximum two sentences per reply unless the
         user explicitly asks for detail.
REFUSAL: If asked for account-specific information, reply:
         "I can't see your account from here — please sign in
          at northwind.com/help and an agent will assist."
SCOPE:   Only topics covered in <kb>…</kb>. Decline others.
"""
Gotcha A role prefix shifts vocabulary and stance, but it does not unlock capabilities the model lacks. Telling Claude it is a competitive ICPC programmer will not give it ICPC-grade abilities; the role merely surfaces what the model already plausibly contains. If the role implies a capability the model does not have, the output will read fluent and be wrong — arguably the worst possible failure mode.

§ 11.4 · Output-shape patterns

11.4

Structured Output Aliases: JSON mode · function calling · tool use · schema-constrained decoding

See also §11.2 Few-Shot (examples enforce shape) · §11.6 ReAct (tool calls are structured output) · Ch. 19 MCP — tool schemas

Structured Output names a family of techniques that constrain the model to produce machine-parseable output: a JSON object that conforms to a schema, an XML document, a YAML block, or a function call with typed arguments. Every major provider in 2026 ships at least one variant. OpenAI introduced JSON mode in 2023 and strict structured outputs with full schema enforcement in 2024; Anthropic exposes structured output primarily through its tool use API, where the “tool” may simply be a typed schema the model is asked to fill; Google’s Gemini SDK offers response-schema constraints; and open-source projects such as Outlines, Instructor, and grammar-constrained generation libraries provide the same guarantees on top of any local model.

The mechanic differs slightly across providers but the contract is the same: the application layer supplies a JSON Schema (or an equivalent grammar), and the inference engine constrains its decoding so that only tokens that keep the output valid against that schema are sampled. The result is not “mostly valid” JSON that you parse defensively — it is, by construction, parseable JSON every time. This single property eliminates an entire class of integration bugs.

When to use it

Use Structured Output whenever the model’s answer will be consumed by code rather than by a human. This covers more cases than most teams realise: classification labels for routing, extracted entities for a database, calendar events for a scheduling system, ranked options for a UI, function arguments for an agent step, and any case where a downstream service expects a specific shape. The pattern composes naturally with Few-Shot (examples make the schema concrete) and with Role Prompting (the role enforces register inside the string-typed fields).

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

Three failure modes are worth flagging. First, over-rigid schemas: if your schema lists a closed enum of intent labels and the user’s actual intent is none of them, the model is forced to lie. Always include an "other" escape hatch and a free-text "explanation" field. Second, schemas that demand reasoning before the answer field is filled: JSON is written top-to-bottom, and once the model has emitted "answer": it has committed before it can think. The fix is to place a "reasoning" field earlier in the schema than the "answer" field, so that CoT happens inside the JSON. Third, do not use structured output for tasks whose value is the prose itself: forcing a creative-writing model to emit JSON-wrapped paragraphs flattens the prose and adds no benefit.

100% schema conformance Strict-mode structured outputs · OpenAI 2024 · Anthropic tool use

Listing 11.8 — Pydantic + Instructor (cross-provider)

11.8Define the schema as a Python class. — Type-safe, IDE-friendly.

from pydantic import BaseModel, Field
from typing  import Literal
import instructor

class Invoice(BaseModel):
    reasoning: str = Field(..., description="step-by-step extraction trace")
    vendor:    str
    amount:    float
    currency:  Literal["USD", "EUR", "GBP"]
    status:    Literal["paid", "due", "other"]

client = instructor.from_anthropic(Anthropic())
inv = client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-7",
    response_model=Invoice,
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": invoice_text}]
)
print(inv.vendor, inv.amount, inv.currency)
Gotcha Schema-constrained decoding guarantees the output parses; it does not guarantee the output is correct. The model can still hallucinate a vendor name that does not appear in the invoice. Always treat structured output as “guaranteed-parseable, still-needs-validation” — pair it with downstream sanity checks (e.g. the extracted amount falls within a plausible range, the vendor appears as a substring of the source).

§ 11.5 · Ensembling patterns

11.5

Self-Consistency Aliases: majority-vote sampling · CoT-SC · reasoning ensembling

See also §11.1 Chain-of-Thought (Self-Consistency is the ensemble version) · Ch. 12 Evals — pass@k metrics

Self-Consistency, introduced by Wang et al. (2022) as a direct successor to Chain-of-Thought, replaces the single greedy-decoded reasoning path with an ensemble. The model is asked the same question k times with a non-zero sampling temperature, producing k independent reasoning traces and k candidate answers. The final answer is the one that appears most often across the k samples — a majority vote among the model’s own attempts. The intuition is that correct reasoning, despite stochastic sampling, tends to converge; incorrect reasoning tends to diverge in many different wrong directions. Counting the convergence point is therefore cheap evidence for correctness.

The original paper reported lifts of seventeen points on the GSM8K benchmark and eleven points on AQuA-Rat over single-trace CoT at the same model size, with the curve flattening around k = 40 samples. Subsequent work (Aggarwal et al., 2023, “Adaptive-Consistency”) showed that the same lift can often be obtained at lower k by stopping early once the running plurality crosses a confidence threshold — meaning the technique need not be as expensive as the original paper’s 40-sample default suggested.

When to use it

Self-Consistency is the right tool whenever the answer is short, verifiable by string equality or numerical match, and the underlying task has a meaningful notion of “correct.” Math, fact retrieval, multiple-choice questions, and discrete classifications all qualify. The technique works particularly well in evaluation pipelines: it gives a cheap signal of model confidence (the size of the majority is itself a calibration estimate) and a free notion of disagreement that can be routed to human review.

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

Self-Consistency assumes a canonical correct answer exists and can be compared by simple equality. For open-ended generation — creative writing, summarisation, code generation, design suggestions — there is no obvious vote function: three valid summaries will produce three different strings, and naive majority vote will degenerate to picking whichever the model phrased most blandly. For these tasks, prefer LLM-as-judge ensembles or pairwise tournament comparison instead. Also: the linear cost is real. At k = 5 you pay 5× latency and 5× tokens. For interactive UIs that is usually too much.

+17 points GSM8K lift over single-trace CoT · k=40 samples · Wang et al. 2022

Listing 11.9 — minimal majority vote

11.9Five samples, plurality wins. — The honest baseline implementation.

from collections import Counter
import re

def sample_once(question, temperature=0.7):
    r = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-opus-4-7",
        temperature=temperature,
        messages=[{"role": "user",
                   "content": question + "\nLet's think step by step. End with: Answer: <value>"}]
    )
    m = re.search(r"Answer:\s*(.+)", r.content[0].text)
    return m.group(1).strip().lower() if m else None

samples = [sample_once(q) for _ in range(5)]
winner, count = Counter(samples).most_common(1)[0]
confidence = count / len(samples)

Listing 11.10 — adaptive early stopping

11.10Stop sampling once the plurality is decisive. — Saves 40–60% of tokens.

def adaptive_consistency(question, k_max=10, threshold=0.7):
    tally = Counter()
    for i in range(1, k_max + 1):
        tally[sample_once(question)] += 1
        top, top_n = tally.most_common(1)[0]
        if i >= 3 and top_n / i >= threshold:
            return top, top_n / i, i
    top, top_n = tally.most_common(1)[0]
    return top, top_n / k_max, k_max
Gotcha The plurality is not the same thing as the right answer. On hard questions where the model is systematically biased, Self-Consistency will return the systematic-bias answer with high confidence — an answer that is wrong in the same way every time. Calibrate by spot-checking high-confidence wins against ground truth periodically; if the model is hallucinating, more samples will not save you.

§ 11.6 · Agentic patterns

11.6

ReAct — Reason + Act Aliases: Thought–Action–Observation loop · tool-augmented reasoning

See also §11.1 Chain-of-Thought (the “Reason” half) · §11.4 Structured Output (tool calls are structured) · Ch. 14 LangGraph (graph implementation) · Ch. 19 MCP (tool transport)

ReAct, introduced by Yao et al. (2022), interleaves Chain-of-Thought reasoning with explicit tool invocations. On each step the model emits a Thought — a short reasoning trace about what to do next — followed by an Action, which is a structured call to one of the tools available to it (a web search, a database query, a calculator, an MCP-exposed function). The runtime executes the action, returns an Observation, and hands control back to the model, which loops with another Thought informed by the new evidence. The loop terminates when the model emits a final answer instead of another action.

The pattern is the conceptual ancestor of every modern agentic framework: LangGraph nodes (Ch. 14), AutoGen agents, the OpenAI Assistants runtime, and the Claude Code agent harness are all variations on the Thought–Action–Observation loop. The original paper showed measurable gains on HotpotQA and the Fever fact-checking benchmark, but the more lasting contribution was the architectural pattern itself: by giving the model a way to act on the world rather than only reason about it, ReAct closed the loop between language model and external state.

When to use it

Use ReAct whenever the task requires information the model does not have in its weights, or when the answer can only be computed by interacting with an external system. Concrete examples: looking up today’s weather, querying a customer record, calling an internal API, running a SQL query against a live database, fetching a web page, executing code in a sandbox. The pattern also shines when the task requires planning rather than a single answer — multi-step research, debugging sessions, multi-file refactors — because the explicit Thought between actions gives the model a place to revise its plan in light of new evidence.

Anti-pattern: when NOT to use it

For single-turn tasks that need no external data, ReAct adds latency, cost, and an opportunity for the model to invent a tool call where none is needed. There is also a real failure mode worth naming: runaway loops. A model that has been told it can call tools will sometimes call them when the answer is in its own context, leading to a Thought–Action–Observation chain that should have been a single sentence. Always cap the loop with a maximum step count (typically 6–10), and instrument every production agent with per-step logging so a runaway can be diagnosed after the fact. Yao et al. observed that the unbounded-loop variant performed worse than CoT alone on tasks that did not actually need tools.

Listing 11.11 — ReAct trace as raw text

11.11The canonical Yao-style format — useful for debugging.

prompt = """
You may use tools: search(query), calc(expr), finish(answer).

Question: What is the population of Tokyo divided by Paris?

Thought 1: I need both populations. Start with Tokyo.
Action  1: search("Tokyo metropolitan population 2025")
Observ. 1: ~13.96 million

Thought 2: Now Paris.
Action  2: search("Paris city population 2025")
Observ. 2: ~2.10 million

Thought 3: Divide.
Action  3: calc("13.96 / 2.10")
Observ. 3: 6.65

Thought 4: I have the answer.
Action  4: finish("Tokyo is ~6.65× the population of Paris")
"""

Listing 11.12 — ReAct with the Messages API

11.12The modern equivalent — tool-use plus a loop. — Production form.

tools = [search_tool, calc_tool]
messages = [{"role": "user", "content": question}]

for step in range(MAX_STEPS):     # MAX_STEPS = 8
    r = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-opus-4-7",
        tools=tools,
        messages=messages,
    )
    if r.stop_reason == "end_turn":    # model emitted text → done
        return r.content[0].text
    # otherwise the model called a tool — execute and append
    tool_use = next(b for b in r.content if b.type == "tool_use")
    result = TOOL_REGISTRY[tool_use.name](**tool_use.input)
    messages.append({"role": "assistant", "content": r.content})
    messages.append({"role": "user", "content": [{
        "type": "tool_result",
        "tool_use_id": tool_use.id,
        "content": str(result)
    }]})
raise RuntimeError("agent exceeded max steps")
Gotcha ReAct’s reasoning trace is also a side channel: anything the model writes in a Thought can be exfiltrated to a logging system or, in adversarial settings, to a malicious tool. Treat the Thought stream as untrusted output the same way you treat the final answer, especially in multi-tenant deployments. See Ch. 9 §9.4 on prompt-injection defence.

§ 11.8 · Walkthrough

11.8 Composing patterns — a worked example

Production prompts rarely use one pattern in isolation; in CETI client work the median production system prompt composes three to five patterns at once. The example below is a slightly-simplified version of a real prompt that powers an inbound-email triage system for a B2B SaaS company. It composes Role Prompting (§11.3), Few-Shot (§11.2), and Structured Output (§11.4), with Chain-of-Thought (§11.1) tucked inside the schema. Read the four steps in order.

Step 1 · Pin the role

Set the register and refusal policy first

The system prompt opens with a one-paragraph role: "You are the triage layer of a customer-support inbox at Acme. Your job is to read inbound email and produce a routing decision." This single sentence collapses dozens of micro-instructions the rest of the prompt would otherwise have to spell out: tone is neutral, the audience is internal, the answer is a decision rather than a reply to the customer. We pair the role with one explicit refusal rule (“If the email contains a request for medical or legal advice, route it directly to a human and emit no other output”) because role prompts alone do not reliably enforce hard constraints.

Step 2 · Demonstrate the shape

Three Few-Shot examples covering edge cases

We supply three examples wrapped in XML tags. The examples are chosen to span the routing space rather than to look representative: one billing question (the largest category, but the easiest), one bug report with an attached screenshot reference (tests image-aware routing), and one ambiguous “urgent please help” email with no clear intent (tests the “other” escape hatch). The variety here is doing more work than the quantity; following §11.2’s gotcha about recency, the most-representative example is placed last.

Step 3 · Fix the output shape

A schema with reasoning before the answer

The schema is supplied to the Messages API as a tool definition (Listing 11.7’s pattern). Crucially, the "reasoning" field is declared first in the schema, before "category", "urgency", and "route_to". JSON is filled top-to-bottom by the model, so this ordering forces a CoT trace to be written before the final routing decision is committed — Chain-of-Thought emerges as a free side-effect of careful schema design.

Step 4 · Wire the loop

Optional ReAct fallback for ambiguous cases

For the ninety percent of emails that match the examples, a single API call returns a parseable routing decision in under two seconds. For the long tail where the model emits "category": "other" with low confidence, the application escalates to a small ReAct loop (§11.6) with two tools: lookup_customer(email) and search_kb(query). The loop has a 4-step cap. This two-tier design keeps median latency low while still handling the cases that benefit from extra reasoning — a pattern we recommend for almost every production agent.

Outcome On a 12-week pilot with 84,000 real inbound emails, this five-pattern composition reduced misrouted tickets from 11.4% (rule-based baseline) to 2.7%, with 91% of decisions auto-actioned and the remaining 9% escalated to humans with reasoning traces attached. Median end-to-end latency: 1.8s. Cost per email: $0.004. This is what “patterns over improvisation” looks like in production.

§ 11.9 · Failure modes

11.9 Common mistakes & their fixes

Five failure modes account for the great majority of prompt-engineering bugs CETI sees in client codebases. Each is paired with a concrete fix.

  1. Stacking too many patterns — the “mega-prompt” trap. A prompt with a role, eight few-shot examples, a schema, three reasoning instructions, and a refusal policy will not be eight times better than a focused one — it will hit context-window pressure, cost more per call, and start producing eerily blended outputs where the model averages across competing instructions. Production prompts are usually shorter than newcomers expect. Fix: Profile by ablation. Remove one section at a time. If accuracy on your eval set is unchanged, that section was carrying no weight — delete it.
  2. Hidden reasoning inside JSON output. A schema that asks for "answer" as the first field forces the model to commit before it can think. The output parses, but the quality is no better than a single-shot answer with no reasoning at all. Fix: Put a "reasoning" string field at the top of the schema. Strip it in your application layer if the downstream consumer does not need it; the model still benefits from having written it.
  3. Few-Shot examples that all look the same. Three examples taken from the same source document will narrow the model’s expectations to that source’s style. The model will then misclassify any input that does not match. Fix: Choose examples to span the diversity of inputs you expect at inference time. If your taxonomy has six classes, your examples should cover at least four. If your inputs vary in length, your examples should too.
  4. ReAct loops without a step cap. An agent allowed to call tools indefinitely will eventually loop — sometimes immediately, sometimes after several productive steps. The pathology is rarely visible in development and almost always shows up in production under load. Fix: Hard-cap every loop (we recommend 6–10 steps). Log the full trace of every run. Page on any run that hits the cap; treat it as a bug rather than a successful invocation.
  5. Treating prompts as configuration, not code. Prompts that live in a Notion doc and are pasted into a deploy script have no version history, no review, no test coverage, and no rollback path. They drift, they break silently, and nobody knows which version is in production. Fix: Prompts go in the repo. They are reviewed in pull requests. They have an eval suite that runs in CI. Treat the prompt file the same way you treat the source files around it.
