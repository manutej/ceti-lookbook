If you can’t measure it, you can’t ship it.

A field guide to AI evaluation in 2026 — the four metrics that actually matter, the four ways to measure them, the six tools competing for your budget, and a walkthrough from zero to first eval. Built for teams that have to defend their model choices to a CFO.

01 · The four metrics that matter

Everything else is a derivative.

A mature evals program tracks dozens of signals. But every dashboard worth keeping reduces to four primary axes — accuracy, faithfulness, latency, and cost. Optimize one in isolation and you will degrade the other three. The art is in the composite: a single Monday-morning view where all four move together or not at all.

01

Accuracy

Does the output match the right answer? On labeled data, this is exact-match, F1, BLEU, or semantic similarity above threshold.

accuracy = correct / total

When it matters: classification, extraction, code generation, structured outputs, any task with a defensible ground truth.

How it’s gamed: overfit to the eval set; pick a metric the model already wins; ignore long-tail buckets that drag the mean down.

02

Faithfulness

Is every claim in the output supported by the context the model was given? Scored by an LLM judge or by an entailment classifier (NLI).

faithfulness = supported_claims / total_claims

When it matters: RAG, summarization, agents that cite sources. The single best leading indicator of hallucination risk in production.

How it’s gamed: a judge that’s too lenient; passing only the chunks that match; rewriting the prompt to suppress claims rather than verify them.

03

Latency

Time-to-first-token (TTFT) and total response time. Track P50, P95, P99 — the long tail is what ruins UX, not the mean.

P95 = sort(times)[0.95 × n]

When it matters: interactive chat (TTFT < 400ms feels alive), voice agents (< 800ms end-to-end), and any tool-using agent where loops compound.

How it’s gamed: measuring mean instead of P95; cherry-picking off-peak windows; excluding cold-start or queueing time.

04

Cost

Dollars per useful action — not per token, not per request. Tie the cost to a business unit so it travels into the P&L.

cost = ($/in × in_tok + $/out × out_tok) / actions

When it matters: always. The gap between $0.30 and $3.00 per resolved support ticket is the difference between a deployable feature and a science project.

How it’s gamed: hiding retries, ignoring embedding spend, leaving cached test traffic in the denominator, forgetting reasoning-token surcharges.

03 · Methodology, part 1

§1 — Ground-truth evals

Ground-truth evaluation is the oldest, cheapest, and most defensible form of model evaluation. You have a labeled dataset where each input has a known correct output. You run your system on every input. You compare. The aggregate score is your accuracy. The same harness runs on every commit, every model upgrade, every prompt change.

The math is unglamorous and that is the point. For classification: accuracy = correct / total. For ranked retrieval: recall@k = relevant_in_top_k / total_relevant. For free-text against a reference: BLEU, ROUGE-L, BERTScore, or a sentence-transformer cosine similarity above a calibrated threshold. For code: pass-rate on a held-out unit-test suite, which is exactly the HumanEval and MBPP recipe.

MMLU-Pro is the canonical academic example. 12,032 multiple-choice questions across 14 domains, ten options each (vs. the original MMLU’s four), filtered to remove answerable-by-elimination items. The score is plain accuracy. GPT-4 class models sit around 70–76%; Claude 3.7 Sonnet and Gemini 2.5 are in the same band. Frontier scores have clustered inside a 2-point spread for nearly a year, which is exactly why your internal eval set matters more than ever.

Code: a minimal lighteval run

# pip install lighteval[accelerate]
from lighteval.main_accelerate import main
from lighteval.tasks.requests import Doc

main(
  model_args="pretrained=meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct",
  tasks="leaderboard|mmlu_pro|0|0",
  output_dir="./results",
  num_fewshot_seeds=1,
  max_samples=500,
)
# Result: accuracy = 0.412 ± 0.022 (95% CI)
Tip

Build your own MMLU-Pro of one domain. Take 200 representative inputs from your last quarter of production traffic. Have two domain experts label expected outputs independently. Throw out the items they disagree on. You now have a defensible, private, drift-resistant eval set worth more than any public benchmark.

The known weakness: ground-truth evals are brittle to phrasing. “Paris” and “Paris, France” should both be right. Exact-match says no. The fix is either a semantic-similarity threshold or — when stakes are high — a judge model that returns a binary equivalent / not equivalent verdict per row. Which is exactly the bridge to §2.

When to invest in your own labeled set

Public benchmarks tell you whether a model is generally competent. They cannot tell you whether it is competent at your task, in your domain, on your customers’ phrasing. The break-even point arrives faster than teams expect: if you ship more than two model or prompt changes per quarter, a 200-item private eval pays back its labeling cost on the first regression it catches. Treat the eval set as production infrastructure — version it in git, code-review changes to it, document why each item exists.

The second non-obvious benefit: a labeled set forces clarity about what the product is actually for. Teams routinely discover, mid-labeling, that they cannot agree on the right answer to questions their users send every day. That disagreement is the most valuable output of the entire exercise — it surfaces a product decision that was never made, just assumed.

04 · Methodology, part 2

§2 — LLM-as-judge

When there is no ground truth — open-ended generation, summarization, agent trajectories, multi-turn chat quality — you ask a second, usually stronger, model to grade the first one’s output. Done well, an LLM judge reaches roughly 80% agreement with human raters, which is the same agreement humans reach with each other. Done badly, it is a confidence machine that ratifies the same failure modes you were trying to detect.

A good judge prompt is short, specific, and gives the judge an explicit rubric and an explicit scale. Avoid free-form numerical scoring (“score from 1 to 10”) — judges cluster on 7. Prefer binary or three-point Likert per rubric item, and combine outside the model.

Code: a faithfulness judge

from anthropic import Anthropic
client = Anthropic()

JUDGE_PROMPT = """You are evaluating whether a RAG answer is FAITHFUL
to the source passages.

Source passages:
{context}

Question: {question}
Answer: {answer}

For each claim in the Answer, decide if it is fully supported
by the Source passages. Output JSON:
{{
  "supported_claims": int,
  "total_claims": int,
  "unsupported": [str, ...],   // verbatim quotes
  "verdict": "PASS" | "FAIL"   // PASS iff supported == total
}}"""

def judge(context, question, answer):
    msg = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-opus-4-5",
        max_tokens=512,
        temperature=0,           # judges run at temp=0
        messages=[{"role": "user",
                   "content": JUDGE_PROMPT.format(...)}],
    )
    return json.loads(msg.content[0].text)

Calibrating the judge

An uncalibrated judge is a liability. Before trusting a judge in production, validate it the same way you would validate any classifier:

  • Build a calibration set. 50–100 examples a human expert has graded PASS/FAIL with reasons.
  • Run the judge against the calibration set. Compute precision, recall, and Cohen’s κ vs. the human labels. Aim for κ > 0.6 before shipping. Below 0.4, your judge is noise.
  • Iterate the rubric. Disagreements are gold — they almost always reveal an ambiguous rubric, not a stupid judge.
  • Re-validate quarterly. Judge models update. Your rubric and your eval set should both have version numbers.

The four known failure modes

Anthropic, OpenAI, and the Berkeley LMSYS team have all published the same list. Memorize it:

  • Position bias. In pairwise comparison, judges prefer whichever answer appears first. Fix: randomize order, or evaluate each candidate independently and combine outside the model.
  • Verbosity bias. Judges prefer longer answers. Fix: include a length-penalty in the rubric, or normalize by token count.
  • Self-preference. Judges prefer outputs from their own model family. Fix: use a different family for judging than for generation (Claude judging GPT, Gemini judging Claude).
  • Sycophancy. If the prompt hints at a preferred answer, the judge agrees. Fix: judge prompts contain only the rubric and the data — never the author’s expectation.
Warning

Never use the same model as both producer and judge. The agreement number you get back is a self-portrait, not an evaluation. Pair across families — GPT-4o producing, Claude judging, or vice versa.

05 · Methodology, part 3

§3 — Human-in-loop

Human evaluation is the slowest, most expensive, and most authoritative form of model evaluation. You will never run it on every output. You run it on a sample, on a schedule, and you use it to calibrate the cheaper signals that run continuously. In regulated domains — medical, legal, financial, safety — it is non-negotiable.

The unit of human eval is the labeling guideline: a written specification of what counts as PASS, what counts as FAIL, with worked examples of edge cases. A 5-page guideline you have iterated on is worth ten times what a 1-page guideline straight from the PM’s head is worth. If two annotators read the same guideline and disagree on the same item, the guideline is wrong, not the annotators.

Inter-annotator agreement

Cohen’s kappa (κ) measures agreement above chance between two raters on categorical labels. The formula:

κ = (P_observed − P_chance) / (1 − P_chance)

Interpretation, from Landis & Koch (1977), still standard:

  • κ > 0.80 — almost perfect agreement; your guideline is sharp.
  • 0.60–0.80 — substantial; ship the rubric, monitor disagreements.
  • 0.40–0.60 — moderate; rewrite the rubric, retrain annotators.
  • < 0.40 — the task itself is ambiguous; rescope before labeling more.

Cost & cadence

Budget realistically. A domain expert grades roughly 30–60 items per hour at $75–$250 per hour fully loaded, depending on specialty. That puts a high-confidence weekly sample (200 items, two graders, one adjudicator) at $1,500–$4,000 per week. Compared to a public incident, it is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

Pattern

Stratified sampling beats random. Don’t grade 200 random outputs — grade 50 from each of: high-confidence PASS, low-confidence PASS, judge-flagged FAIL, and user-thumbs-down. You will catch four times more real bugs per dollar.

The three jobs human review actually does

If you cannot answer “what is this human reviewer producing that no other signal produces?” the program will be cut in the next budget cycle. There are three defensible answers:

  • Calibration target. Human grades are the ground truth your LLM judge is measured against. Without them, your judge’s κ number is fiction.
  • Edge-case detection. The judge will confidently approve a class of failures it was never trained to catch. Humans surface those classes; you then update the rubric.
  • Regulatory artifact. In healthcare, legal, and financial domains, a signed human review is the evidence trail an auditor will ask for. Build the workflow as if the auditor is already on the way, because eventually she is.

Tooling for human review

Most teams start in a shared spreadsheet, hit limits at around 100 items per week, and graduate to a purpose-built labeling tool. Argilla and Label Studio are the OSS defaults; Scale Rapid and Surge are the SaaS choices when you need a managed labeler pool. The single feature that matters: adjudication workflow — when two annotators disagree, a senior reviewer makes the final call, and that final call is what enters the dataset.

One pattern that consistently outperforms the rest: shadow-grade your own production. Once per week, pick yesterday’s 50 lowest-confidence outputs, grade them as a team in a one-hour standing meeting, and discuss every disagreement on the spot. The meeting doubles as rubric maintenance, judge calibration, and on-call rotation training. Teams that adopt it stop arguing about quality in slack; the meeting is where that argument now lives.

06 · Methodology, part 4

§4 — A/B testing in production

Offline evals tell you whether a change could be better. A/B testing tells you whether it is better, for your real users, in your real product, with all the messiness those words imply. Everything else is a leading indicator; the A/B test is the truth.

Three deployment patterns

Pick the pattern that matches the blast radius of the change:

  • Shadow deploy. The new version runs on 100% of traffic but its output is never shown to the user. You log both responses, compare offline, and ship if the new one is at least as good. Zero user risk, full statistical power, but you pay for two inferences per request.
  • Canary / gradual rollout. 1% → 5% → 25% → 100% over days or weeks. Cuts inference cost vs. shadow, but a regression at 5% hits 5% of users.
  • Holdout test. 95% see the new version, 5% stay on the old indefinitely. Lets you measure long-horizon effects (retention, repeat usage) that a one-week A/B will miss.

Online metrics that matter

Offline scores rarely map cleanly to outcomes. Build the bridge with a small panel of online metrics chosen for the product, not the model:

  • Task completion rate. Did the user accomplish what they came for? For chat, did the conversation end without escalation?
  • Thumbs / explicit feedback rate. Noisy — only a few percent of users rate — but the trend line is robust.
  • Implicit signals. Copy-events, retry rate, regeneration rate, average session length, return-within-7-days. These are the leading indicators of real quality.
  • Business KPI. The one that pays for the team — deflection rate for support, conversion for sales, retention for chat, accepted-completion rate for coding.

The single most-cited cautionary tale: a coding assistant team shipped a model that scored 4 points higher on HumanEval but had a lower acceptance rate in production. The new model was more verbose in its explanations, which broke the IDE’s autocomplete grammar. Offline up, online down. The A/B test caught it; the offline eval did not.

Statistical sanity before claiming a win

A 0.4-point improvement in a 200-user A/B test is noise wearing a suit. Before declaring victory, three checks:

  • Power calculation up front. Decide the minimum effect size that matters — usually 1–2% on the primary metric — and compute how many users you need to detect it at 80% power, α = 0.05. Most teams overestimate their own traffic by 3× and run underpowered tests for months.
  • Bayesian over frequentist when traffic is low. A Beta-Binomial posterior gives you a continuous “probability that B beats A” number that’s easier to reason about than a p-value, and degrades gracefully with small N.
  • Pre-register the metric. Write the primary metric, the guardrail metrics (the ones a regression on cancels the win), and the stopping rule before you launch. If you find yourself slicing by Tuesday vs. Wednesday to find a win, you are HARKing — hypothesizing after results known.
Pattern

Always pair an online A/B with a continuous offline eval. The A/B tells you what users do; the offline eval tells you why. Without both, your retrospective on every regression turns into a guessing game.

How to actually pick

The decision matrix collapses to four questions, in this order:

  • Does your data need to stay on your infrastructure? If yes — healthcare, finance, EU customers under GDPR/AI Act — you are choosing between Arize Phoenix self-hosted and Helicone self-hosted. Skip the rest.
  • Is the team already on LangChain or LangGraph? If yes, LangSmith will be the path of least resistance for the first six months. You can leave later; the OTel exports are clean.
  • Do you want eval and observability as one product, or two? One: Braintrust. Two: Helicone for traces + your judge harness of choice for evals (DeepEval, promptfoo, or OpenAI Evals).
  • Do you need pre-built, defensible judges? If hallucination detection is the headline KPI — legal-tech, medical scribes, customer support at scale — Patronus AI’s Lynx and GLIDER ship with published F1 scores against public hallucination benchmarks. You will pay enterprise prices.
Note

OpenTelemetry is becoming table stakes. By end of 2026, every serious observability vendor will accept OTel spans for LLM calls (OpenLLMetry-compatible). Instrument once, swap vendors freely. Lock-in via proprietary SDKs is a 2024 problem; in 2026 it should be a deal-breaker.

08 · Walkthrough

Setting up evals from scratch — 7 steps.

A real procedure, not a sales deck. Follow it end-to-end in a single afternoon. By Friday you have a baseline; by next Friday you have a defensible improvement.

1

Pick 20 representative examples.

Pull 20 inputs from production logs in the last 30 days. Stratify across your top-3 user intents. If you don’t have logs yet, write 20 from a PM’s memory and an engineer’s — you can rotate them out once real traffic arrives.

2

Label expected outputs by hand.

Two people label independently. Compare. Discuss the disagreements — those discussions are how the rubric gets written. Throw out items where you can’t agree after 5 minutes.

# dataset.jsonl
{"id": "q1", "input": "What's your refund policy?",
 "expected": "30 days, full refund, no questions asked.",
 "must_contain": ["30 days", "refund"]}
3

Write the judge prompt.

One judge per rubric item. Binary verdict. Temperature 0. Use a different model family than the one you’re evaluating. See §2 for the failure modes to avoid.

4

Calibrate the judge against humans.

Run the judge on the labeled set. Compute Cohen’s κ vs. the human labels. If κ < 0.6, rewrite the rubric and re-run. Do not proceed past this step with a noisy judge.

5

Run the baseline.

Run your current production system against the 20 examples. Record accuracy, faithfulness, P95 latency, and cost. This is the number every future change has to beat.

import braintrust
from autoevals import Factuality

braintrust.Eval(
    "support-rag",
    data=lambda: load_jsonl("dataset.jsonl"),
    task=lambda q: rag_pipeline(q),
    scores=[Factuality, FaithfulnessJudge, Latency],
)
6

Iterate one variable at a time.

Change the prompt OR the model OR the retrieval depth — never two at once. Run. Compare to baseline. Keep the change only if it wins on the metric that matters without regressing the others by more than 1σ.

7

Wire the eval into CI.

Block merges that regress accuracy by > 2% or P95 latency by > 15%. From now on, eval failures show up next to test failures, in the same PR comment, with the same authority.

09 · Common pitfalls

Five ways a real evals program goes wrong.

1

Overfitting to the eval set.

You iterate on prompts until your 50-item eval is 100%. Production accuracy doesn’t move. The eval became a training target. Fix: hold out 20% as a sealed test set you only run quarterly; rotate items in and out monthly so no single item dominates.

2

Evaluation drift.

The world changes — product features ship, user intents shift, the underlying model gets silently updated — but your eval set is frozen at v1. Fix: version your eval set; refresh 10% of items monthly from recent production logs; re-baseline after every model upgrade.

3

Ignoring user-perceived quality.

Every eval passes. Every metric is green. Users still complain. The eval covers correctness but not tone, latency feel, or recovery from confusion. Fix: add an “experience” track — thumbs ratio, retry rate, regeneration rate — that runs alongside the correctness track and is reviewed weekly.

4

Trusting an uncalibrated judge.

You ship a judge with κ = 0.3 against human labels — barely better than coin flip. The judge then confidently approves regressions. Fix: never deploy a judge below κ = 0.6; re-validate quarterly; treat the judge itself as a model under evaluation.

5

Optimizing one axis in isolation.

Accuracy is up 4 points. Cost is up 6×. Latency P95 is up 800ms. Net product impact: negative. Fix: publish a single composite score — e.g. accuracy × (1 / cost) × (1 / latency) — that the team agrees has to move together. Optimize the composite.

10b · Organizational maturity

Where on the curve is your team?

Five stages, measured not by tooling but by what the team can answer in a Monday meeting. Most production AI teams in 2026 are at stage 2 or 3 and don’t know it.

0

Vibes-driven.

No dashboard, no eval set. Quality is “feels good in demos.” Regression detection is a user complaint. Every model upgrade is a leap of faith. Most pre-launch teams live here, which is fine — until they ship.

1

Spreadsheet evals.

A PM maintains a Google Sheet of 30 example inputs and grades outputs manually after each prompt change. The signal is real; the workflow does not scale beyond one person and breaks the day she goes on PTO.

2

Automated offline evals.

The 30 examples live in a JSONL file. A script runs them on every commit. Accuracy and latency are tracked over time. Engineers can self-serve a baseline before opening a PR. This is the inflection point where eval discipline starts paying back its cost.

3

Judges + production traces.

An LLM judge calibrated to κ > 0.6 grades a daily sample of real traffic. Faithfulness, relevance, and helpfulness move on a shared dashboard. The team has a weekly review meeting that surfaces both regressions and product bugs the engineers wouldn’t have found.

4

Closed loop with A/B and humans.

Every change ships behind a feature flag with a pre-registered A/B test. Human review feeds the eval set monthly. The judge is re-calibrated quarterly. Offline scores, online metrics, and human grades all line up — or the discrepancy is the next quarter’s research project. Fewer than 10% of production AI teams operate here in 2026.
