01

A Short Manifesto · Why Patterns

Agentic systems are no longer one-off. Reusable structures matter.

Two years ago, building an AI feature meant writing one heroic prompt and praying. That era is over. The 2024 inflection wasn't a smarter model — it was the realisation that production AI systems are compositions of smaller decisions: classify before answering, split before combining, plan before executing, critique before shipping.

What used to be a craft of intuition is now a discipline of structures. A handful of repeatable moves cover virtually every shipped agent today. Knowing the moves is the difference between a demo that wows on Tuesday and a system that runs at 3 a.m. on a holiday weekend.

This lookbook names six patterns. Each one is a constraint — a deliberate refusal to do everything in one shot. Constraints are where reliability lives. The pattern is not the dish; it is the technique that makes the dish possible. Learn the techniques, and the architecture stops being a mystery.

We borrow the canonical four from Anthropic's December 2024 Building Effective Agents essay — Chaining, Routing, Parallelization, Orchestrator-Workers — and add a fifth that is too often left out of the textbook canon: Evaluator-Optimizer, the generator-critic loop that turns a good first draft into a shippable answer. The sixth move is the meta-move: composition — using several patterns inside one system, the way a chef uses several techniques inside one menu.

Stop reaching for "an agent." Reach for the right pattern — then compose.

02
Why Patterns? — A Library Of Techniques

Building With AI Isn't One Thing.

A great chef doesn't improvise every dish from scratch. They lean on a small library of techniques — braise, sear, reduce, fold — and combine them per recipe. The technique isn't the dish; it's the move that makes the dish possible.

Building agentic AI works the same way. The teams shipping reliable systems aren't inventing the wheel each time — they're composing reusable structures that decide how work flows between AI models, when to split it up, and who synthesises the result. Learn them, and most "AI architectures" suddenly become legible.

The Four Canonical Patterns — Adapted From Anthropic, Dec 2024

Four Ways To Put AI Models To Work Together.

01 PROMPT
CHAINING

A Step-By-Step Assembly Line

Each AI step hands its output to the next, like cooks at a station.

You break a job into a fixed sequence: outline first, draft second, polish third. Slower than a single shot, but each step is simpler and the system is easier to debug.

02 ROUTING

A Receptionist With The Right Specialist

Classify the question first, then send it to the expert who handles that kind.

The first step classifies the request, then forwards it to a specialist. The benefit isn't speed — it's precision. A team of focused specialists outperforms one generalist.

03 PARALLEL
IZATION

Three Reviewers Reading At Once

Fan the task out, work simultaneously, and combine the answers at the end.

Run the same prompt (or different ones) at the same time, then merge. Use it when subtasks are independent — or when you want multiple opinions voting on the safest answer.

04 ORCHESTRATOR
WORKERS

A Foreman Hiring Crew On The Fly

A lead AI inspects the job, decides how many helpers to spin up — and what each does.

The most flexible — and the hardest to keep on rails. A lead AI reads the request, decides on the fly how many sub-tasks to spawn, dispatches them, and stitches the answers back together.

§02 — Deep Dive · Pattern 01

Prompt Chaining — The Assembly Line.

Chaining is the simplest agentic pattern and the one teams should default to. You decompose a task into a fixed sequence of steps, where each step's output becomes the next step's input. You sacrifice latency to buy two things teams chronically underprice: reliability and debuggability.

Use When

The task has a clean, predictable structure. You can write down the steps in advance. Each step has a verifiable intermediate output. You care more about quality than speed.

Do Not Use When

The task is genuinely unpredictable. You'd be paying for sequential round-trips when a single well-prompted call would suffice. Latency is a hard constraint (sub-second).

The Move

Each link in the chain does one thing well. A failed step is isolable — you can re-run just the broken link instead of regenerating the whole pipeline. Add a programmatic gate between steps (a "guardrail check") to fail fast on bad intermediates.

Why It Works

Smaller prompts are more reliable. A 50-line prompt that does five things has five chances to go wrong; five 10-line prompts each have one. You also unlock cheaper models for cheaper steps — the extraction step might run on Haiku, the reasoning step on Sonnet.

Step → Step → Step
Anchor: Yellow square — the gate-keeper colour. Each block is a checkable intermediate.
// Skeleton — Three-Step Chain # Step 1: extract structured data from raw input data = extract(raw_input, model="haiku") # Programmatic gate — fail fast if extraction broke assert data.is_valid(), "extraction failed — abort chain" # Step 2: reason over the structured data analysis = analyze(data, model="sonnet") # Step 3: format for the end user final = format_report(analysis, model="haiku") return final
Real Example 01 · Data Extraction Pipeline
Invoice → Ledger

An accounting bot processes scanned invoices. Step 1: OCR-extract line items into JSON. Step 2: classify each line into a chart-of-accounts code. Step 3: validate totals match. Each step is auditable; finance teams trust a chain they can inspect, not a black box.

Real Example 02 · Content Generation
Brief → Outline → Draft → Polish

A long-form marketing pipeline takes a creative brief and runs four passes: outline (structure), draft (prose), fact-check (verification), polish (style). The polish step uses the brand voice guide; the fact-check step refuses to ship if citations don't resolve.

§03 — Deep Dive · Pattern 02

Routing — The Specialist Switchboard.

Routing puts a classifier at the front door. Every incoming request is read, categorised, and dispatched to the specialist prompt or model that handles that category best. You stop trying to be everything to everyone in a single prompt and instead operate a team of focused experts.

Use When

Your input space has discrete, recognisable categories. The categories need genuinely different handling. You see quality drop on a "one big prompt" approach as you add more capabilities.

Do Not Use When

The category boundaries are fuzzy or context-dependent. You only have two or three categories (a conditional inside one prompt is simpler). The classifier itself is unreliable.

The Move

Write a small, sharp classifier prompt that emits one of N labels. Each label maps to a specialist — a different prompt, a different model, a different toolset, or all three. Specialists never see requests outside their lane, so their prompts stay short and their behaviour stays predictable.

Why It Works

Specialisation reduces prompt bloat. A refund specialist doesn't need to know about API errors. A bug-triage specialist doesn't need refund policy. Each specialist's context window is spent on signal, not on disclaimers. Add a "fallback" route for low-confidence classifications — usually escalating to a human or a larger model.

Classify → Dispatch
Anchor: Red triangle — the directional cut. One door in, many doors out.
// Classification Prompt — Skeleton classifier_prompt = """ You are a triage classifier. Read the user request and emit EXACTLY ONE of these labels — no explanation, no quotes: REFUND - billing reversal or charge dispute BUG_REPORT - product behaving incorrectly HOW_TO - usage question / documentation ACCOUNT - login, profile, subscription change ESCALATE - anything else, or low confidence Request: {user_input} Label:""" label = classify(classifier_prompt, model="haiku") match label: case "REFUND": return refund_agent(user_input) case "BUG_REPORT": return bug_agent(user_input) case "HOW_TO": return docs_agent(user_input) case "ACCOUNT": return account_agent(user_input) case _: return human_handoff(user_input)
Real Example 01 · Support Triage
One Door, Five Lanes

An e-commerce support bot routes refund vs. bug vs. how-to vs. account vs. escalate. The refund specialist has access to the payments API; the bug specialist can search known issues; the docs specialist can search the help centre. None of them are tempted to answer outside their lane.

Real Example 02 · Model Routing
Cheap First, Smart On Demand

A coding assistant routes "explain this snippet" to Haiku and "refactor this 400-line file" to Sonnet. The classifier itself runs on Haiku. Result: 4× cheaper at unchanged user-perceived quality, because the hard tasks still get the smart model.

§04 — Deep Dive · Pattern 03

Parallelization — Fan Out, Combine.

Parallelization runs multiple model calls simultaneously and aggregates the results. It comes in two distinct flavours that solve different problems — they share a shape but not a purpose. Treating them as one pattern is a common mistake.

Variant A · Sectioning

Break a task into independent subtasks and run them in parallel. Each subtask is a different question; you concatenate the answers. Use when the work decomposes cleanly and the parts don't need to see each other. Example: summarising a long report by running per-chapter summaries concurrently, then concatenating.

Variant B · Voting (Self-Consistency)

Run the same task multiple times and pick the answer that wins a majority vote (or has the highest aggregate score). Use when correctness matters more than cost — content moderation, vulnerability detection, anything where a single false positive or false negative is expensive.

Use When

Subtasks are genuinely independent (sectioning). Or correctness is critical and you can afford 3-5× the inference cost (voting). Latency is bounded by the slowest call, not the sum.

Do Not Use When

Subtasks depend on each other's outputs (use chaining). The task is easy enough for one call (voting is wasteful). You're trying to fix systemic prompt issues with more samples (won't help).

The Aggregation Step Matters

Voting requires a deterministic merger: majority vote, mean score, "any worker flagged → flag overall." Sectioning requires a writer prompt to weave per-section outputs into a coherent whole. The aggregator is the load-bearing piece — get it wrong and parallelism makes things worse, not better.

Fan-Out · Merge
Anchor: Blue field — the parallel plane. Three workers, one aggregator.
// Variant B — Voting / Self-Consistency # Run the same evaluation 5 times in parallel results = parallel([ evaluate(content, temperature=0.7) for _ in range(5) ]) # Majority vote — flag only if 3+ workers agree flags = [r.is_violation for r in results] if sum(flags) >= 3: return {"action": "block", "confidence": sum(flags)/5} else: return {"action": "allow", "confidence": (5-sum(flags))/5}
Real Example 01 · Long-Document Summary
Sectioning In Practice

A legal-doc summariser splits a 200-page contract into 12 sections, summarises each in parallel (12 concurrent Haiku calls), then a final Sonnet pass weaves the section summaries into an executive brief. Latency: 12 seconds total instead of 90.

Real Example 02 · Content Safety Voting
Five Reviewers, One Verdict

A platform runs every borderline post through five independent moderation calls at varying temperatures. A post is blocked only if at least three flag it. False-positive rate dropped 40% without retraining the classifier — and false negatives also fell, because each worker catches edge cases the others miss.

§05 — Deep Dive · Pattern 04 · The Deepest One

Orchestrator — Workers Spawned On Demand.

Orchestrator-Workers is the most powerful and the most dangerous pattern in the canon. A lead model reads the request, dynamically decides how to break it into sub-tasks (the structure isn't fixed in advance), spawns workers for each, and synthesises their outputs. Unlike parallelization — which uses a fixed set of pre-defined subtasks — the orchestrator plans at runtime.

The defining property is dynamic decomposition. The orchestrator doesn't know ahead of time whether it will spawn three workers or thirty, what each will do, or what tools they'll need. That flexibility is the entire reason to reach for this pattern — and the entire reason it's hard to keep on rails.

Use When

The structure of the work depends on the input. Coding agents (where the file changes depend on the bug). Research agents (where the sources depend on the question). Anything where "fixed sequence of steps" would force you to either over-engineer or under-serve.

Do Not Use When

A simpler pattern would work. The orchestrator's planning is itself unreliable. You can't afford the latency of "plan → spawn → wait → synthesise → maybe re-plan." You don't have observability into worker calls.

The Worked Example · Research Agent

A research agent is the canonical case. Ask it "compare these three fleet-electrification incentive programs" and it can't follow a pre-baked recipe — it must inspect each program, decide what sub-questions matter, spawn a worker per source, gather, and write.

The orchestrator's job is fourfold: (1) plan — emit a list of sub-tasks tied to specific sources; (2) dispatch — spawn a worker per sub-task, often with different tools (web search, document loader, code interpreter); (3) monitor — handle worker failures, retries, re-planning if a source is bad; (4) synthesise — weave the worker outputs into one coherent answer with citations.

Guardrails Are Mandatory

Hard limits prevent disaster: maximum worker count (cap at 10-20), maximum recursion depth (orchestrators that spawn orchestrators are a rabbit hole), per-worker timeout, token budget (the orchestrator's spend can spiral fast), and structured output for the plan step so it's machine-parseable and validatable before any worker is spawned.

Observability Is The Real Cost

You will spend more on logging, tracing, and replay tooling than on the orchestrator itself. Every worker invocation must be inspectable. Every plan revision must be diffable. Without this, debugging is impossible and trust never gets established.

Plan · Spawn · Synthesise
Anchor: Black field — the runtime planner. White lead, blue workers, yellow synthesis bar.
// Skeleton — Research Orchestrator def research_agent(question: str) -> str: # 1. PLAN — orchestrator emits structured sub-tasks plan = llm( f"Decompose this research question into 3-8 sub-tasks. " f"Each sub-task names a specific source and a specific extraction goal. " f"Output JSON: [{{ 'source', 'goal', 'tool' }}, ...]\nQ: {question}", response_format=SubTaskList, max_subtasks=8, ) # 2. DISPATCH — one worker per sub-task, in parallel, with timeout findings = parallel([ worker(t, timeout=30) for t in plan.subtasks ]) # 3. MONITOR — handle failures, optionally re-plan if failure_rate(findings) > 0.3: return replan_or_abort(question, findings) # 4. SYNTHESISE — weave findings into one cited answer return llm( f"Synthesise these findings into a single answer to: {question}\n" f"Include citations. Findings: {findings}" )
Real Example · Coding Agent
The File-Editing Loop

A coding agent given "add OAuth to this Flask app" can't pre-plan exactly which files change. It reads the codebase, plans the file-by-file edits, spawns a worker per file to draft changes, runs tests, and re-plans if tests fail. The loop is the value.

Real Example · Research Agent
Multi-Source Synthesis

The lookbook you're reading was built this way: the orchestrator decomposed "agentic patterns" into sections, spawned a worker per pattern (each with its own context), collected the drafts, ran an editor pass for voice, and laid out the final document. Different sources, one voice.

§06 — Deep Dive · Pattern 05 · Often Left Out Of The Canon

Evaluator × Optimizer — Generator Meets Critic.

Evaluator-Optimizer is the most under-taught pattern in the canon. One model generates; another model critiques; the generator revises; the loop continues until the critic is satisfied (or a max-iteration cap is hit). It is the closest agentic equivalent of "draft, get edits, revise."

Use When

You can articulate clear evaluation criteria but can't easily encode them as a single prompt. Quality matters more than latency. The task benefits from explicit critique — code, copywriting, structured documents.

Do Not Use When

The criteria are subjective and the critic can't agree with itself. The generator can't actually improve from feedback (lookup tasks, factual recall). You can't cap the loop — convergence is not guaranteed.

The Critic Is The Whole Game

A weak critic generates noise: "make it better" doesn't help. A strong critic emits structured, actionable feedback: which test failed, which constraint was violated, which sentence was unclear. The discipline is in the critic's prompt, not the generator's — invest there.

Worked Example · Code Generation With Refinement

A code-writing agent generates a function, runs it through a critic that checks against a test suite plus style rules, and revises until tests pass or the loop hits its cap. The output of the critic isn't a vibe — it's specific diffs the generator must address. Most production coding agents use some form of this loop, often invisibly.

Generate ↔ Critique
Anchor: Yellow field — the feedback loop. Black generates, red critiques.
// Skeleton — Code Generator + Critic Loop def refine(spec, tests, max_iters=5): code = generate(spec) for i in range(max_iters): critique = critic( code=code, tests=tests, # critic MUST emit structured, actionable feedback response_format=Critique, ) if critique.passes_all_tests: return code # revise — generator sees specific failures, not "do better" code = revise(code, critique.failures) return code # cap reached — return best-so-far + warning

§08 — Real-World Compositions · Three Vignettes

No Shipped System Uses One Pattern Alone.

In production, patterns nest. A router fronts an orchestrator. A chain ends in an evaluator. A parallelizer feeds a synthesiser. Below are three composition sketches drawn from systems that work today.

01 Customer Support

The Tier-1 Support Agent That Almost Never Escalates

The front door is a router: refund, bug, how-to, account, escalate. Most categories route to a specialist with a focused prompt. The "bug" lane is different — it routes into an orchestrator, because debugging a customer's issue may require pulling logs, checking incident reports, querying their account state, and reading the relevant docs page.

The orchestrator decides which of those tool-calls to make based on the symptoms. The final answer runs through an evaluator that checks whether the response actually addresses the user's question and includes a next-action — if not, it bounces back for one revision before being sent.

02 Document Analysis

The 200-Page-Contract Pipeline That Finishes In Twelve Seconds

A legal-tech pipeline ingests long contracts. Stage one is a chain: structure-extraction (clauses, parties, dates) then a manifest of what to analyse. An orchestrator decomposes "research electric trucks for our fleet" into per-source workers: one for total cost of ownership, one for charging logistics, one for federal and state incentives, one for vendor reliability data. §09 — Anti-Patterns · Five Failure Modes With Fixes

The Five Mistakes Teams Make On The Way To Production.

01

Reaching For An Orchestrator First

Teams default to "let's build an agent" when chaining or routing would do the job in 1/10th the complexity and 1/3rd the latency.

Fix: Try the simplest pattern that could possibly work. Only upgrade when you can name what the simpler one is missing.
02

No Guardrails On The Loop

Orchestrator and evaluator-optimizer loops can spiral — spawning workers indefinitely or critiquing forever. Token bills explode. Latency unbounded.

Fix: Always cap max workers, max recursion, max iterations, per-step timeout, total token budget.
03

Weak Critic, Strong Generator

In an evaluator-optimizer loop, a vague critic ("be better") wastes inference. The generator can't act on critique that isn't structured and specific.

Fix: Invest in the critic's prompt. Force structured output. Test the critic on known-bad examples before trusting the loop.
04

Skipping Observability Until It's Too Late

Multi-step systems are unfixable without inspection. Teams add tracing after the first 3 a.m. incident — too late.

Fix: Log every prompt, every model output, every tool call from day one. Trace IDs across patterns. Replay-by-trace-ID early.
05

Treating Sectioning And Voting As One Pattern

"Parallelization" is two patterns with opposite purposes — sectioning splits a task, voting repeats it. Conflating them produces bad architecture and worse decisions.

Fix: Name which variant you're using and why. Use voting only when correctness matters more than cost.
