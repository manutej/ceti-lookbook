01 — Why patterns?

Building with AI isn't one thing.

A great chef doesn't improvise every dish from scratch. They lean on a small library of techniques — braise, sear, reduce, fold — and combine them per recipe. The technique isn't the dish; it's the move that makes the dish possible.

Building agentic AI works the same way. The teams shipping reliable AI systems aren't inventing the wheel each time. They're composing four reusable structures — patterns that decide how work flows between AI models, when to split it up, and who synthesises the result. Learn the four, and most "AI architectures" suddenly become legible.

02 — The four canonical patterns

Four ways to put AI models to work together.

Adapted from Anthropic's "Building Effective Agents" (Dec 2024). Hover any tile to see it light up.

Prompt Chaining PATTERN 01

A step-by-step assembly line.

Each AI step hands its output to the next, like cooks at a station.

Routing PATTERN 02

A receptionist with the right specialist.

Classify the question first, then send it to the expert who handles that kind.

Parallelization PATTERN 03

Three reviewers reading at once.

Fan the task out, work simultaneously, and combine the answers at the end.

Orchestrator-Workers PATTERN 04

A foreman hiring crew on the fly.

A lead AI inspects the job, then decides how many helpers to spin up — and what each one does.

Prompt Chaining

You break a job into a fixed sequence: outline first, draft second, polish third. Each step's output feeds the next. Slower than a single shot, but each step is simpler and the system is easier to debug — like a kitchen line where every cook owns one task.

Routing

The first step classifies the request, then forwards it to a specialist designed for that category. The benefit isn't speed — it's precision. A team of focused specialists outperforms one generalist trying to handle everything in a single prompt.

Parallelization

Run the same prompt (or different ones) at the same time, then merge the results. Use it when subtasks are independent — like three reviewers reading a contract — or when you want multiple opinions to vote on the safest answer.

Orchestrator-Workers

You don't know in advance how to split the work. A lead AI reads the request, decides on the fly how many sub-tasks to spawn, dispatches them to worker AIs, and stitches the answers back together. The most flexible — and the hardest to keep on rails.

04 — What this looks like in practice

Three systems you've probably already used.

Each one is built on a pattern from above. The pattern is invisible until you know to look.

Customer Support

The triage desk that never sleeps.

Modern support bots don't try to answer every question with one giant prompt. The first model reads your message, decides if it's a refund, a bug, or a billing question, and hands it to a specialist trained on that lane.

your message → classifier → specialist agent
Document Analysis

The contract-reader, in three passes.

When AI reviews a long contract, it usually isn't one heroic read. Pass one extracts the structure. Pass two flags risky clauses. Pass three writes the executive summary — each pass building on the last.

extract → flag risks → summarize
Research Assistant

The analyst who hires their own team.

Ask an AI to "research electric trucks for our fleet" and a lead model decides what's needed: a worker for total cost, one for charging logistics, one for incentives. It pulls the threads together into a single brief.

lead AI plans → spawns workers → synthesizes brief
